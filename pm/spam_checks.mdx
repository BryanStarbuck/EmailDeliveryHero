# EmailDeliveryHero — Deliverability checks catalog

This is the **master catalog of every health check** EmailDeliveryHero runs against a monitored
domain to measure email deliverability — whether mail authenticates, whether the domain or its
sending IPs are on blacklists, whether the sending infrastructure is sane, and whether message
content and sending patterns look like a legitimate sender. It is the product's core reference: the
app's whole promise is that every problem it finds comes with the **exact fix**.

Read this alongside the sibling specs and do not duplicate them:

* [Overview](./overview.mdx) — the charter, the personas, and the first-round check list.
* [Engineering](./engineering.mdx) — the pluggable `Checker`/`Finding` contract, the registry +
  runner, the file store, and the periodic scheduler that every check below plugs into.
* [Dashboard](./dashboard.mdx) — how the six category cells are rendered, colored, and sorted
  (the scoring roll-up summarized here is specified there in full).
* [Domain on a blacklist](./use_cases/domain_on_blacklist.mdx) — the end-to-end walkthrough of a
  DNSBL finding and its delisting remediation.

Every check in this catalog is one implementation of the `Checker` interface from
[Engineering §6.1](./engineering.mdx): it returns zero or more `Finding` objects, each with a
`severity` of `ok | info | warning | critical` and — for anything that is not `ok` — a concrete
`remediation` string (the exact record to publish or the step to take). Adding a check is adding one
file.

The primary checker per category: `spfCheck` (`spf/spf.check.ts`), `dkimCheck` (`dkim/dkim.check.ts`), `dmarcCheck` (`dmarc/dmarc.check.ts`), `blacklistCheck` (`blacklist/blacklist.check.ts`), the `infra.*` family (`mx-routing/`, `reverse-dns/`, `tls-transport/`, `mta-sts/`, `tls-rpt/`, `dane-tlsa/`, `dnssec/`, `dns-health/`), and the `content.*` family (`content-scoring/content-scoring.check.ts` — `contentScoringCheck`, plus `bimi/`, `link-url-reputation/`, `list-unsubscribe/`, `inbox-placement/`, `report-emails/`). ## The six categories (the dashboard columns)

Every sub-check in this document belongs to **exactly one** of six categories. These are the six
columns of the Dashboard, and each is a group of many sub-checks that collapses into a single cell
(e.g. "2 of 7 fail"):

| # | Category | `checkId` prefix | What it answers |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **SPF** | `spf` | Is Sender Policy Framework published correctly and within limits? |
| 2 | **DKIM** | `dkim` | Are DKIM keys published, parseable, and strong? |
| 3 | **DMARC** | `dmarc` | Is a DMARC policy published, enforcing, aligned, and reporting? |
| 4 | **Blacklists** | `dnsbl` | Are the domain or its sending IPs listed on any DNSBL? |
| 5 | **DNS & Infrastructure** | `infra` | Is the mail-serving DNS/transport layer resolvable, reachable, and secure? |
| 6 | **Spam & Content** | `content` | Do the messages, headers, and sending patterns look like a legitimate sender? |

Relevant standards referenced throughout: **SPF — RFC 7208**; **DKIM — RFC 6376** (key strength
**RFC 8301**); **DMARC — RFC 7489**, now obsoleted by the **DMARCbis** set **RFC 9989/9990/9991**
(published May 2026, backward-compatible with `v=DMARC1`); **ARC — RFC 8617**; **MTA-STS —
RFC 8461**; **SMTP TLS Reporting (TLS-RPT) — RFC 8460**; **DANE/TLSA — RFC 6698 / RFC 7671**;
**List-Unsubscribe one-click — RFC 8058** (with the older List-Unsubscribe header from RFC 2369);
**BIMI** (IETF draft; VMC/CMC issued by Mark Verifying Authorities).

## 1. SPF — Sender Policy Framework

**What it measures.** SPF (RFC 7208) is a DNS TXT record that lists which hosts are authorized to
send mail using the domain in the SMTP `MAIL FROM` (envelope-from). Receivers check it to decide
whether a message's envelope sender is legitimate. SPF is also the SPF half of DMARC alignment, so a
broken SPF record silently degrades DMARC pass rates and pushes mail to spam. The most common
real-world failure is not "no SPF" but a record that **exceeds the 10-DNS-lookup limit** and
therefore evaluates to `PermError` — which many receivers treat as a fail.

**How measured.** First round: `resolveTxt(domain)` via `node:dns/promises`, then parse and simulate
the record's mechanism graph, recursively resolving `include`/`a`/`mx`/`redirect` to count lookups.
A `dig txt` fallback (Homebrew) is a future enhancement for cache-busting.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `spf.present` | Exactly one `v=spf1` TXT record exists at the apex. | TXT lookup at domain. | **critical:** no `v=spf1` record found. | Publish a TXT record at the apex: `v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all` (adjust the include for your ESP). |
| `spf.single` | Not more than one SPF record (multiple SPF records is a hard `PermError` per RFC 7208 §4.5). | Count `v=spf1` TXT records. | **critical:** two or more `v=spf1` records → invalid, SPF ignored. | Merge into one record; delete the extra TXT so exactly one `v=spf1` remains. |
| `spf.lookups` | The record resolves within the **10 DNS-lookup limit** (`include`, `a`, `mx`, `ptr`, `exists`, `redirect` count; `ip4`, `ip6`, `all` do not). | Recursively expand and count DNS-querying mechanisms. | **critical:** > 10 lookups → `PermError`; **warning:** 8–10 (near the ceiling). | Reduce lookups: replace `a`/`mx` with explicit `ip4:`/`ip6:` ranges, drop unused ESP includes, or flatten the record (see `spf.flatten`). |
| `spf.void` | Not more than **2 void lookups** (mechanisms whose DNS query returns NXDOMAIN/empty), per RFC 7208 §4.6.4. | Track empty/NXDOMAIN answers while expanding. | **warning/critical:** ≥ 2 void lookups (stale include to a decommissioned ESP). | Remove `include:`/`a`/`mx` entries that point at domains with no SPF/records. |
| `spf.all` | The record ends with a single `all` mechanism and its qualifier is sane: `-all` (fail) or `~all` (softfail); **never `+all`**. | Parse the terminal `all` token. | **critical:** `+all` (authorizes the whole internet); **warning:** no `all`, or `?all` (neutral). | Use `-all` once coverage is confirmed, or `~all` during rollout. Remove any `+all`. |
| `spf.ptr` | The deprecated `ptr` mechanism is not used (RFC 7208 §5.5 deprecates it; it is slow and unreliable). | Scan mechanisms for `ptr`. | **warning:** `ptr` mechanism present. | Delete the `ptr` mechanism; replace with explicit `ip4:`/`ip6:` or `include:`. |
| `spf.syntax` | The record parses: valid terms, valid CIDR, no stray characters, correct macro syntax (`%{...}`). | Tokenize and validate against the RFC 7208 grammar. | **critical:** malformed term or bad macro → `PermError`. | Fix the offending term; validate the corrected record before publishing. |
| `spf.length` | No single TXT string exceeds 255 chars and the record is served without needing excessive concatenation. | Inspect TXT chunk lengths. | **warning:** record split awkwardly / near DNS UDP limits. | Split long records into 255-char strings correctly, or flatten to shorten. |
| `spf.flatten` *(advisory)* | Flags records that would benefit from flattening (many nested includes with volatile upstreams). | Heuristic on lookup count + include depth. | **info:** record is fragile / near the limit. | Adopt TTL-aware SPF flattening (manual first round; automated flattening service is a future enhancement). |

> External tooling note: all SPF sub-checks are **pure DNS** and ship first round. Flattening
> automation, if added later, is the only piece that may need a third-party API.

## 2. DKIM — DomainKeys Identified Mail

**What it measures.** DKIM (RFC 6376) attaches a cryptographic signature to outbound mail; the
public key lives in DNS at `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` and receivers use it to verify the
message was not altered and really came from the domain. DKIM is the more robust half of DMARC
alignment (it survives forwarding, unlike SPF). A missing, unparseable, or weak key means messages
fail DKIM, weakening DMARC and reputation.

**How measured.** First round: `resolveTxt('<selector>._domainkey.<domain>')` for each selector in
`CheckContext.selectors`, then parse the key record (`v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=<base64>`) and inspect the
decoded public key's modulus length. Selector discovery beyond the configured list (probing common
selectors like `google`, `default`, `selector1`, `k1`, `s1`) is a future enhancement.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `dkim.present` | For each configured selector, a `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` TXT record exists. | TXT lookup per selector. | **critical:** no DKIM record for a selector known to sign mail. | Publish the public key your ESP/MTA generated at `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` as a TXT record. |
| `dkim.parses` | The record is a valid DKIM key: has `v=DKIM1` (or defaulted), a `k=` type, and a non-empty `p=` public key that base64-decodes. | Parse tags; base64-decode `p=`. | **critical:** `p=` empty (revoked key) or record does not parse. | Republish the correct public key from the signing system; an empty `p=` means the key is revoked. |
| `dkim.keylength` | RSA key modulus is **≥ 1024 bits (required)** and **≥ 2048 bits (recommended)** per RFC 8301. | Decode the public key and read the modulus size. | **critical:** < 1024-bit; **warning:** exactly 1024-bit (upgrade advised). | Generate a **2048-bit** key, publish the new selector, re-sign, then retire the old selector. |
| `dkim.testflag` | The record is **not** in test mode (`t=y` tells receivers to ignore DKIM results). | Read the `t=` flag. | **warning:** `t=y` present in production. | Remove `t=y` from the DKIM TXT record once testing is complete. |
| `dkim.algorithm` | The key/hash algorithm is modern (`k=rsa`; avoid SHA-1; `ed25519` allowed alongside RSA). | Read `k=` and `h=` tags. | **warning:** deprecated/weak algorithm advertised. | Reissue with RSA-SHA256 (or add an ed25519 selector alongside RSA for compatibility). |
| `dkim.multi` *(advisory)* | At least one working selector exists and, ideally, a second is staged for rotation. | Count valid selectors. | **info:** only one selector (no rotation headroom). | Stage a second selector so keys can be rotated without an outage (M3AAWG: rotate at least every 6 months). |
| `dkim.rotation` *(advisory)* | Key age / rotation cadence, when the selector's publish date is known. | Metadata heuristic (config-tracked). | **info:** key older than the rotation window. | Rotate the DKIM key on schedule; keep the old selector live briefly during cutover. |

> External tooling note: presence/parse/keylength are **pure DNS** and ship first round. True
> per-message signature verification (did a real message actually pass DKIM?) requires capturing a
> sent message — a future enhancement using `swaks` to send a probe plus a validating receiver.

## 3. DMARC — Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance

**What it measures.** DMARC (RFC 7489, now DMARCbis RFC 9989) ties SPF and DKIM together with
**alignment** and tells receivers what to do with mail that fails (`none` / `quarantine` /
`reject`), plus where to send **aggregate (`rua`) and failure (`ruf`) reports**. It is the single
most load-bearing record for both anti-spoofing and inbox placement, and — since the 2024
Gmail/Yahoo bulk-sender rules — a published DMARC record is effectively mandatory for senders.

**How measured.** First round: `resolveTxt('_dmarc.<domain>')`, parse the tag list, and evaluate the
policy, alignment, and reporting tags. Ingesting the `rua` aggregate XML reports to measure real
pass rates is a future enhancement (needs a mailbox/endpoint + parser).

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `dmarc.present` | A `_dmarc.<domain>` TXT record exists and starts with `v=DMARC1`. | TXT lookup at `_dmarc.<domain>`. | **critical:** no DMARC record. | Publish `v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@<domain>` to start, then tighten. |
| `dmarc.single` | Exactly one DMARC record (multiple = ignored by receivers). | Count `v=DMARC1` records. | **critical:** two or more DMARC records. | Delete the extra TXT so exactly one `v=DMARC1` record remains. |
| `dmarc.policy` | A valid `p=` policy is present and is **enforcing** (`quarantine` or `reject`). | Read the `p=` tag. | **warning:** `p=none` (monitor-only, no protection); **critical:** missing/invalid `p=`. | Move to `p=quarantine` then `p=reject` after confirming legitimate mail passes in `rua` reports. |
| `dmarc.subdomain` | Subdomain policy `sp=` is set (or intentionally inherits `p=`) so subdomains are covered. | Read the `sp=` tag. | **warning:** enforcing `p=` but `sp=none` leaves subdomains spoofable. | Add `sp=reject` (or `sp=quarantine`) to cover subdomains. |
| `dmarc.pct` | `pct=` (if present) is sane; note `pct` is **deprecated in DMARCbis** and should be removed at the next edit. | Read the `pct=` tag. | **info/warning:** `pct` < 100 during rollout, or lingering `pct` after DMARCbis. | Remove `pct` (DMARCbis) or, during a legacy staged rollout, raise it toward 100. |
| `dmarc.rua` | Aggregate reporting is enabled (`rua=mailto:...`) so the owner gets visibility. | Read/parse the `rua=` tag. | **warning:** no `rua` → flying blind on auth failures. | Add `rua=mailto:dmarc@<domain>`; authorize external report receivers with the `<report-domain>._report._dmarc` record if using a third party. |
| `dmarc.ruf` *(advisory)* | Failure/forensic reporting `ruf=` is considered (privacy-sensitive; adoption is limited). | Read the `ruf=` tag. | **info:** no `ruf` (optional). | Optionally add `ruf=mailto:...` with `fo=1` if you want per-failure samples. |
| `dmarc.alignment` | Alignment mode is understood: `adkim`/`aspf` = relaxed (`r`, default) vs strict (`s`); strict can break otherwise-passing mail. | Read `adkim`/`aspf` tags. | **warning:** strict alignment with subdomain senders → alignment failures. | Use relaxed alignment (`adkim=r; aspf=r`) unless you require exact-domain matching. |
| `dmarc.syntax` | The record parses: known tags, valid values, correct ordering of `v=`/`p=`. | Tokenize and validate tags. | **critical:** malformed record ignored by receivers. | Fix the malformed tag; `v=DMARC1` and `p=` must come first and be valid. |
| `arc.present` *(advisory)* | Note whether ARC (RFC 8617) chains are in use for forwarded mail (informational; ARC is applied by intermediaries, not published in a simple DNS record). | Advisory metadata. | **info:** forwarding paths may break DMARC without ARC. | For mailing lists/forwarders, ensure the forwarder applies ARC so downstream receivers can honor prior authentication. |

> External tooling note: policy/alignment/reporting-tag checks are **pure DNS** and ship first
> round. Measuring actual DMARC pass rates from `rua` XML is a future enhancement (report-ingestion
> endpoint or paid DMARC-analytics API).

## 4. Blacklists — DNSBL / IP & domain reputation

**What it measures.** Whether the domain's **sending IPs** (IP-based DNSBLs) or the **domain itself
and the domains in message links** (domain-based RHSBLs) appear on well-known blocklists. A listing
on a widely-consumed list like Spamhaus ZEN can cause outright rejection at many receivers, so this
is frequently the direct cause of "email suddenly started bouncing." See the full walkthrough in
[Domain on a blacklist](./use_cases/domain_on_blacklist.mdx).

**How measured.** For an **IP** listing, reverse the octets and do an `A` lookup of
`<reversed-ip>.<zone>`; a `127.0.0.x` answer means listed, and a `TXT` lookup at the same name
usually returns the reason. Example: to test `203.0.113.24` against `zen.spamhaus.org`, look up
`24.113.0.203.zen.spamhaus.org`. For a **domain** listing (RHSBL), query `<domain>.<zone>` directly
(no octet reversal). All of this is **pure DNS** via `node:dns/promises` and ships first round; the
zone list lives in `config.yaml`.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured (zone) | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `dnsbl.spamhaus_zen` | Sending IPs not on Spamhaus **ZEN** (the combined SBL + CSS + XBL + PBL IP list — the single most impactful zone). | Reversed-IP `A` lookup at `zen.spamhaus.org`; TXT for reason. | **critical:** IP returns `127.0.0.2–11` (listed). | Fix the root cause, then use the **Spamhaus IP/Blocklist Removal Center** to request delisting for that IP. |
| `dnsbl.spamhaus_sbl` | IPs not on the **SBL** (manually confirmed spam sources) — surfaced separately for a precise reason code. | `A` lookup at `sbl.spamhaus.org`. | **critical:** SBL listing. | Stop the confirmed spam source; request SBL removal via Spamhaus with attestation. |
| `dnsbl.spamhaus_xbl` | IPs not on the **XBL** (compromised/exploited hosts, botnets, open proxies). | `A` lookup at `xbl.spamhaus.org`. | **critical:** XBL listing (likely a compromised host). | Find and clean the compromised host/open relay, then request removal. |
| `dnsbl.spamhaus_pbl` | IPs not on the **PBL** (policy list of ranges that should not send direct-to-MX, e.g. dynamic/residential). | `A` lookup at `pbl.spamhaus.org`. | **warning:** PBL listing (sending from an inappropriate range). | Send through a proper smarthost/relay, or, if it is legitimately a static mail server, self-remove via the PBL removal process. |
| `dnsbl.spamhaus_dbl` | The **domain** (and link domains) not on the Spamhaus **DBL** (domain reputation / RHSBL). | Direct `A` lookup of `<domain>.dbl.spamhaus.org`. | **critical:** domain listed on DBL. | Remediate the abused domain (compromised site, spammy links), then request DBL removal. |
| `dnsbl.barracuda` | IPs not on the **Barracuda Reputation Block List (BRBL)**. | `A` lookup at `b.barracudacentral.org` (requires free registration of the resolver). | **critical:** Barracuda listing. | Fix the cause, then submit the **Barracuda IP removal request** form. |
| `dnsbl.spamcop` | IPs not on the **SpamCop Blocking List (SCBL)** (spamtrap/complaint-driven; auto-expires). | `A` lookup at `bl.spamcop.net`. | **critical/warning:** SpamCop listing. | Stop the offending sends; SCBL entries expire automatically once activity stops — confirm via the SpamCop lookup. |
| `dnsbl.sorbs` | IPs not on **SORBS** zones (spam, open relays, dynamic ranges). | `A` lookup at `dnsbl.sorbs.net`. | **warning/critical:** SORBS listing. | Use the SORBS delisting process for the specific sub-zone that listed the IP. |
| `dnsbl.uceprotect` | IPs not on **UCEPROTECT** levels 1–3 (note: L2/L3 are aggressive whole-ASN listings; weight low). | `A` lookup at `dnsbl-1/2/3.uceprotect.net`. | **warning:** UCEPROTECT listing (often collateral, ASN-wide). | Address L1 (your IP) directly; L2/L3 usually require the upstream/ASN to act — weight this lower than Spamhaus. |
| `dnsbl.invaluement` | IPs/domains not on **Invaluement** (ivmSIP / ivmURI) — a respected paid list. | Query the Invaluement zones (subscription/data-feed). | **critical:** Invaluement listing. | Follow Invaluement's removal process; requires a subscription/API for automated checks. |
| `dnsbl.aggregate` | Roll-up across all configured zones: how many zones list any of the domain's IPs/domain. | Combine the per-zone results. | **critical:** listed on ≥ 1 high-impact zone; **warning:** listed only on low-weight zones. | Prioritize delisting from high-impact lists (Spamhaus, Barracuda, SpamCop) first. |

> External tooling note: Spamhaus/SpamCop/SORBS/UCEPROTECT public zones are **pure DNS** and ship
> first round (Barracuda needs the resolving IP registered; high-volume Spamhaus use needs a Data
> Query Service). **Invaluement** and other commercial lists need a paid subscription/API and are a
> later addition. Per-list delisting URLs and reason codes are stored as zone metadata in
> `config.yaml` so each finding hands the user the correct removal tool.

## 5. DNS & Infrastructure

**What it measures.** The transport and DNS plumbing that has to be correct before authentication
even matters: can receivers find your mail servers (MX), does your sending IP have matching forward
and reverse DNS (FCrDNS — a strong reputation signal), is transport encrypted and enforceable
(STARTTLS, MTA-STS, DANE), is there TLS reporting, and are there no dangling or inconsistent
records. Weak infrastructure here quietly caps deliverability and blocks branding features.

**How measured.** Mostly `node:dns/promises` (`resolveMx`, `reverse`, `resolve4/6`, `resolveTxt`,
`resolveNs`, `resolveCname`) plus, for reachability/TLS, a TCP/STARTTLS probe. The MTA-STS policy
file itself requires an **HTTPS fetch** (`https://mta-sts.<domain>/.well-known/mta-sts.txt`), and
DANE/TLSA validation is strongest with **DNSSEC**; both are noted below.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `infra.mx_present` | The domain publishes ≥ 1 MX record with valid priorities. | `resolveMx(domain)`. | **critical:** no MX records (cannot receive mail; bounces & reputation harm). | Publish MX records pointing at your mail host(s) with correct priorities. |
| `infra.mx_resolve` | Each MX hostname resolves to an A/AAAA address (no dangling MX). | Resolve each MX target. | **critical:** MX points at a name with no address. | Fix or remove the MX target so every MX host resolves. |
| `infra.mx_reachable` | Each MX host accepts SMTP on port 25 (and offers a banner). | TCP connect + SMTP banner probe. | **warning:** MX unreachable / connection refused. | Open port 25 / fix the listener so the MX host accepts SMTP. |
| `infra.ptr_present` | Each sending IP has a PTR (reverse DNS) record. | `reverse(ip)`. | **critical:** no PTR (many receivers reject IPs with no reverse DNS). | Ask the IP owner/host to set a PTR for the sending IP. |
| `infra.fcrdns` | **Forward-Confirmed reverse DNS**: the PTR hostname forward-resolves back to the same IP. | `reverse(ip)` then `resolve4/6(ptrHost)`. | **critical/warning:** PTR does not match forward DNS. | Set the PTR to a hostname whose A/AAAA record points back to the same IP. |
| `infra.helo_match` *(advisory)* | The sending host's HELO/EHLO name is an FQDN that matches its PTR/forward DNS. | SMTP banner/EHLO inspection (probe). | **warning:** HELO is a bare/mismatched name. | Configure the MTA to HELO with its real FQDN that matches PTR + A record. |
| `infra.a_aaaa` | The apex/mail hostnames have consistent A and, if used, AAAA records (IPv4/IPv6 consistency). | `resolve4`/`resolve6`. | **warning:** AAAA present but that IPv6 host is unauthenticated/unlisted in SPF. | Either add the IPv6 host to SPF/rDNS or remove the AAAA to avoid unauthenticated IPv6 sends. |
| `infra.starttls` | MX hosts advertise **STARTTLS** and present a valid, non-expired certificate. | SMTP `EHLO` + `STARTTLS` probe + cert inspection. | **warning:** no STARTTLS, or expired/mismatched cert. | Enable STARTTLS with a valid CA certificate whose name matches the MX host. |
| `infra.mta_sts` | An MTA-STS policy exists: `_mta-sts.<domain>` TXT (`v=STSv1; id=...`) **and** the policy file at `https://mta-sts.<domain>/.well-known/mta-sts.txt` (RFC 8461); ideally `mode: enforce`. | TXT lookup + HTTPS fetch of the policy file. | **warning:** no MTA-STS, or `mode: testing` left in place, or TXT/policy mismatch. | Publish the `_mta-sts` TXT and the policy file listing your MX hosts; start in `testing`, then move to `mode: enforce`. |
| `infra.tls_rpt` | A TLS-RPT record exists at `_smtp._tls.<domain>` TXT (`v=TLSRPTv1; rua=...`) per RFC 8460, so TLS-negotiation failures are reported. | `resolveTxt('_smtp._tls.<domain>')`. | **warning:** no TLS-RPT (no visibility into TLS failures). | Publish `_smtp._tls.<domain>` TXT: `v=TLSRPTv1; rua=mailto:tlsrpt@<domain>`. |
| `infra.dane_tlsa` | For DNSSEC-signed domains, TLSA records exist at `_25._tcp.<mx-host>` (DANE, RFC 6698/7671) to bind the MX cert. | `resolveTlsa`/TXT + DNSSEC (AD flag) check. | **info/warning:** DNSSEC present but no TLSA (DANE not deployed); TLSA present without DNSSEC (ineffective). | With DNSSEC enabled, publish TLSA records matching your MX certificates; DANE requires a signed zone. |
| `infra.dnssec` | Whether the zone is DNSSEC-signed (prerequisite for trustworthy DANE and general tamper-resistance). | Check for DS/RRSIG and the resolver AD flag. | **info:** zone not DNSSEC-signed. | Enable DNSSEC at the registrar/DNS provider (publish DS records). |
| `infra.ns_sanity` | Nameservers resolve, are diverse/reachable, and there are no lame delegations. | `resolveNs(domain)` + resolve each NS. | **warning:** single NS, or an NS that does not answer (lame delegation). | Use ≥ 2 reachable nameservers on diverse networks; fix lame delegations. |
| `infra.dangling` | No dangling/obsolete records: CNAMEs to deprovisioned hosts, MX/SPF includes to dead services (subdomain-takeover / spoofing risk). | Resolve referenced targets; flag NXDOMAIN. | **warning/critical:** CNAME or include resolves to an unclaimed/dead target. | Remove the dangling record or reclaim the target; dangling CNAMEs enable subdomain takeover. |
| `infra.autoconfig` *(advisory)* | Client autoconfig/autodiscover hints (`autoconfig.<domain>`, `_autodiscover._tcp` SRV) are present or intentionally absent. | SRV/CNAME/A lookups. | **info:** no autoconfig (mail clients can't auto-set up). | Optionally publish autoconfig/autodiscover records for smoother client setup. |

> External tooling note: MX/PTR/FCrDNS/TXT/NS checks are **pure DNS** and ship first round. The
> **MTA-STS policy fetch (HTTPS)**, **STARTTLS/cert probe (SMTP)**, and **DANE + DNSSEC validation**
> need network probes beyond `node:dns/promises` (an HTTPS client, a TLS-aware SMTP probe, and a
> DNSSEC-validating resolver such as shelling to `dig +dnssec`/`openssl s_client`/`swaks`) — these
> are second-round.

## 6. Spam & Content

**What it measures.** Everything about the **message itself and the sending behavior** that
influences the spam-vs-inbox decision beyond DNS authentication: content/structure scoring
(SpamAssassin-style), required list-management headers, link reputation, brand-indicator (BIMI),
and reputation signals like complaint rate, bounce rate, and sending patterns. This is the category
most aligned with the 2024 Gmail/Yahoo bulk-sender rules and the one most dependent on external
tools and data.

**How measured.** Some sub-checks are **pure DNS/config** (BIMI record, sending-cadence heuristics
from audit history) or **pure local-file** (the report-email corpus analysis). Content scoring
needs a message sample run through **SpamAssassin** (Homebrew
`spamassassin` / `spamc`) or a scoring API; inbox-placement needs a **seed-list** service;
complaint/bounce rates need **feedback loops (FBLs)** and postmaster data. These are flagged as
later additions.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `content.spamassassin` | A sample message scores below the spam threshold. SpamAssassin default is **5.0**; for real inbox safety aim for **0–2**. | Run the message body/headers through `spamassassin`/`spamc` (Homebrew) or a scoring API. | **critical:** score ≥ 5.0; **warning:** score 2–5. | Rewrite the triggering content: fix the rules SpamAssassin cites (spammy phrases, ALL-CAPS subject, broken MIME, missing text part). |
| `content.report_emails` (family — `report_corpus`, `report_domain_attribution`, `report_pass_rate`, `report_spoofing`, `report_fragility`, `report_enforcement`, `report_tls`, `report_freshness`) | What the **receivers' own DMARC aggregate / TLS-RPT reports** say about the domain: real-world pass rate, spoofing, single-mechanism alignment fragility, active quarantine/reject, inbound-TLS failures — analyzed from the report-email corpus on every run, each report attributed to its domain by the payload's policy domain. | Scan the configured report-email directory **in place** (MIME decode, zip/gzip by magic bytes, DMARC-XML / TLS-RPT-JSON parse — [emails.mdx §13](./emails.mdx)); no network. | **critical:** a source failing both SPF+DKIM alignment with real volume, or own mail quarantined/rejected; **warning:** a stream passing DMARC on one mechanism only, pass rate < ~100%, TLS failure sessions, orphan reports. | Authorize/align the named stream (SPF `include:` / DKIM selector), set `aspf=r` or brand the Return-Path for ESP streams, fix the reported TLS layer; add orphan-report domains as monitored domains. |
| `content.subject` | Subject line avoids spam signals: no excessive caps, no leading `RE:`/`FWD:` spoofing, no risky punctuation/emoji stuffing. | Heuristic + SpamAssassin subject rules. | **warning:** subject triggers content rules. | Use a concise, honest subject; avoid ALL-CAPS, excessive `!`, and misleading `RE:`. |
| `content.image_text_ratio` | The message is not image-heavy with little text (image-only mail is a classic spam pattern). | Parse MIME; compute text vs image bytes/area. | **warning:** very low text-to-image ratio (images > ~40% of body). | Add a meaningful plain-text portion; keep images well under ~40% of the message and always include a `text/plain` alternative. |
| `content.multipart` | The message provides a `text/plain` alternative alongside `text/html` and valid MIME structure. | MIME inspection. | **warning:** HTML-only, or malformed MIME. | Send `multipart/alternative` with both plain-text and HTML parts. |
| `content.url_reputation` | Links in the message do not point at domains with poor reputation (RHSBL/URI blocklists such as Spamhaus DBL, Invaluement ivmURI, SURBL). | Extract URLs; query URI blocklists. | **critical:** a linked domain is on a URI blocklist. | Remove/replace the flagged link; if it is your domain, remediate the abuse then request delisting. |
| `content.list_unsubscribe` | Bulk mail includes a `List-Unsubscribe` header (RFC 2369) **and** one-click support via `List-Unsubscribe-Post: List-Unsubscribe=One-Click` (RFC 8058). | Inspect message headers. | **critical:** bulk sender with no one-click unsubscribe (fails Gmail/Yahoo rules). | Add both headers: `List-Unsubscribe: <https://...>, <mailto:...>` and `List-Unsubscribe-Post: List-Unsubscribe=One-Click`, and honor requests within 2 days. |
| `content.precedence` | Bulk/automated mail sets appropriate headers (`Precedence: bulk` where applicable) and does not misuse priority headers. | Header inspection. | **info/warning:** missing/misused precedence or spoofed high-priority flags. | Set `Precedence: bulk` for bulk mail; do not force `X-Priority: 1` on routine mail. |
| `content.from_alignment` | The visible `From:` domain aligns with an authenticated domain (SPF or DKIM) — a Gmail/Yahoo bulk requirement. | Compare `From:` domain vs SPF/DKIM identifiers. | **warning:** `From:` domain does not align with SPF/DKIM. | Send from a `From:` domain that aligns with your DKIM `d=` (or SPF envelope) domain. |
| `content.bimi` | A BIMI record exists at `default._bimi.<domain>` TXT (`v=BIMI1; l=<svg-url>; a=<vmc-url>`); requires DMARC at enforcement. | `resolveTxt('default._bimi.<domain>')` + fetch the SVG/VMC. | **info/warning:** no BIMI, or BIMI without a VMC/CMC (Gmail won't show the logo), or BIMI without `p=quarantine/reject`. | Publish a square SVG Tiny-PS logo and a **VMC/CMC** from a Mark Verifying Authority; BIMI requires DMARC at `p=quarantine` or `p=reject`. |
| `content.seedlist` | Real inbox placement across major providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo) via seed accounts. | Send to a **seed-list** service and read placement (inbox/spam/missing). | **critical:** landing in spam at major providers; **warning:** promotions-tab/mixed. | Address the deliverability factors the seed test exposes (auth, content score, reputation, list hygiene). |
| `content.complaint_rate` | Spam-complaint rate is under the bulk-sender ceiling: keep **< 0.3%**, target **< 0.1%** (Gmail/Yahoo 2024). | Feedback loops (FBLs) + Google Postmaster Tools data. | **critical:** ≥ 0.3%; **warning:** 0.1–0.3%. | Improve list hygiene and consent; remove unengaged recipients; make unsubscribe effortless. |
| `content.bounce_rate` | Hard-bounce rate is low (invalid-address sending damages reputation). | Bounce log / ESP metrics. | **warning/critical:** elevated hard-bounce rate. | Use double opt-in and list validation; remove hard bounces immediately. |
| `content.engagement` *(advisory)* | Engagement signals (opens/clicks, low spam-marks) look like a wanted sender. | ESP/postmaster analytics. | **info:** low engagement / high spam-marks. | Send to engaged recipients; sunset chronically unengaged addresses. |
| `content.warmup` *(advisory)* | Sending volume ramps gradually and stays consistent (no sudden spikes from a cold IP/domain). | Volume/cadence heuristic over audit history + ESP data. | **warning:** sudden volume spike on a cold IP/domain. | Warm up new IPs/domains gradually; keep volume steady day-to-day. |
| `content.feedback_loops` *(advisory)* | The sender is enrolled in provider FBLs so complaints are captured. | Config/attestation check. | **info:** not enrolled in FBLs. | Enroll in provider feedback loops (and Google Postmaster Tools) to receive complaint data. |

> External tooling note: only `content.bimi` and the history-based cadence heuristics are close to
> pure DNS/config first round. **SpamAssassin scoring** needs the Homebrew `spamassassin`/`spamc`
> binary plus a sample message; **seed-list placement**, **complaint/bounce rates**, and **FBLs**
> need third-party services and provider data (Google Postmaster Tools, ESP metrics). Treat this
> whole category as mostly second-round, gated behind those integrations.

## Scoring & how categories roll up to a cell

The Dashboard shows **one cell per category per domain** — six cells across. Each category above
contains many sub-checks, so the runner must collapse a list of `Finding`s into a single **value**
and a single **color**. This mirrors the score/status derivation in
[Engineering §6.2](./engineering.mdx); the full rendering rules live in [Dashboard](./dashboard.mdx),
summarized here so this catalog is self-consistent:

* **Cell value** is the worst-plus-count summary of that category's sub-checks:
  * All sub-checks `ok` → **"Healthy"**.
  * Some fail → a **"N of M fail"** / **"N problems"** summary (e.g. "2 of 7 fail"), optionally as a
    percentage-passing figure for sortability.
  * Never run yet → **"—"** / "Not run".
* **Cell color** is driven by the **worst severity** present in the category:
  * **dark green** — all `ok`.
  * **amber** — worst finding is `warning` (and no `critical`).
  * **dark red** — at least one `critical`.
  * **light gray** — the category has never been run for this domain.
  * (`info` findings do not by themselves turn a cell amber; they surface as advisories.)
* **Domain-level roll-up.** The domain's overall `status` is the worst category color, and its
  numeric `score` (0–100) deducts weighted points per finding (`critical` > `warning` > `info`), so
  the Dashboard is sortable and the periodic scheduler can detect regressions (a category going from
  green to red between runs is a new problem).

Weighting note: not all sub-checks are equal — e.g. a Spamhaus ZEN listing or a missing SPF record
weighs far more than a UCEPROTECT L3 (ASN-wide) listing or a missing `autoconfig` record. The
per-check weights are configuration in the audit engine so the roll-up reflects real-world impact.

## First round vs future

Which sub-checks ship in the first round (pure `node:dns/promises`, deterministic, no external
dependencies) versus which require external tools, network probes, or paid data:

| Category | First round (pure DNS / `node:dns/promises`) | Future (external tool / probe / API / paid data) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SPF** | `spf.present`, `spf.single`, `spf.lookups`, `spf.void`, `spf.all`, `spf.ptr`, `spf.syntax`, `spf.length` | `spf.flatten` automation (flattening service) |
| **DKIM** | `dkim.present`, `dkim.parses`, `dkim.keylength`, `dkim.testflag`, `dkim.algorithm`, `dkim.multi` | Per-message signature verification (`swaks` probe + validator), selector auto-discovery, `dkim.rotation` age tracking |
| **DMARC** | `dmarc.present`, `dmarc.single`, `dmarc.policy`, `dmarc.subdomain`, `dmarc.pct`, `dmarc.rua`, `dmarc.ruf`, `dmarc.alignment`, `dmarc.syntax` | `arc.present` reality (report-derived), real pass-rate measurement from `rua` XML ingestion / DMARC-analytics API |
| **Blacklists** | `dnsbl.spamhaus_*`, `dnsbl.spamcop`, `dnsbl.sorbs`, `dnsbl.uceprotect`, `dnsbl.aggregate` (public DNS zones) | `dnsbl.barracuda` (resolver registration), `dnsbl.invaluement` (paid feed), high-volume Spamhaus via Data Query Service |
| **DNS & Infrastructure** | `infra.mx_present`, `infra.mx_resolve`, `infra.ptr_present`, `infra.fcrdns`, `infra.a_aaaa`, `infra.tls_rpt`, `infra.ns_sanity`, `infra.dangling` | `infra.mx_reachable` + `infra.starttls` (SMTP probe), `infra.mta_sts` (HTTPS policy fetch), `infra.dane_tlsa` + `infra.dnssec` (DNSSEC resolver), `infra.helo_match`, `infra.autoconfig` |
| **Spam & Content** | `content.bimi`, `content.report_*` (report-email corpus analysis — pure local-file, [emails.mdx §13](./emails.mdx)), history-based `content.warmup`/cadence heuristics | `content.spamassassin` (`spamassassin`/`spamc`), `content.subject`/`image_text_ratio`/`multipart` (needs a message sample), `content.url_reputation` (URI blocklists), `content.list_unsubscribe`/`precedence`/`from_alignment` (message headers), `content.seedlist`, `content.complaint_rate`/`bounce_rate`/`engagement`/`feedback_loops` (FBLs, Postmaster Tools, ESP metrics) |

The rule of thumb: **anything answerable from DNS ships first**; anything that needs to send or
inspect a real message, open an SMTP/HTTPS connection, validate DNSSEC, or read a third-party
reputation feed is a later round. In all rounds the `Checker`/`Finding` contract is unchanged — only
a checker's internals differ — so the Dashboard, store, and scheduler never change as checks are
added.
