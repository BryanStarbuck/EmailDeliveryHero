# EmailDeliveryHero — DKIM check

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail, **RFC 6376**) attaches a cryptographic signature to every outbound
message and publishes the matching **public key in DNS** at `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>`.
Receivers hash the signed headers and body, verify them against that public key, and thereby confirm
the message was **not altered in transit** and genuinely originated from the signing domain. DKIM is
the more durable half of DMARC alignment — a DKIM signature survives forwarding and mailing lists,
whereas SPF breaks on the envelope rewrite — so a missing, unparseable, weak, or revoked key quietly
collapses DMARC pass rates, strips the sender of a positive reputation signal, and pushes mail to the
spam folder. Since the **2024 Gmail/Yahoo bulk-sender rules** and **Microsoft's May 2025
outlook.com enforcement** (senders of ≥5,000 msgs/day must pass SPF, DKIM, *and* aligned DMARC or be
rejected/junked), valid DKIM plus DMARC alignment is effectively mandatory for any sender doing
volume.

This check belongs to the **DKIM** dashboard category; all of its sub-check ids use the **`dkim`**
prefix. It audits the **DNS-published key side** of DKIM (what a receiver would fetch to verify a
signature); message-layer verification (does a real signed message actually pass, is `l=` present,
is the signature aligned) is future — the methodology map in §2 shows exactly which layer each
question lives in.

## 1. What it checks

**Background.** A signing MTA/ESP hashes selected headers plus the body of an outbound message and
signs the hash with a **private key**. It stamps a `DKIM-Signature:` header carrying the domain
(`d=`), the **selector** (`s=`), the algorithm (`a=`, e.g. `rsa-sha256`), the signed-header list
(`h=`), the body hash (`bh=`), and the signature (`b=`). The **public key** lives in DNS as a TXT
record at `<selector>._domainkey.<d-domain>`. A verifier reads `d=` and `s=` off the message,
fetches that TXT record, and checks the signature. The selector is just a label, so one domain can
publish **many keys** (one per ESP, one per rotation generation) side by side — and selectors
**cannot be enumerated from DNS** (no wildcard answers `*._domainkey`, no registry), which is why
discovery (§2.2) matters.

**The standards.** **RFC 6376** defines the signature and the key record format (the `v=`, `k=`,
`p=`, `t=`, `s=`, `h=`, `g=`, `n=` tags). **RFC 8301** sets the security floor: RSA keys **must be
at least 1024 bits and should be 2048 bits**, and **SHA-1 (`rsa-sha1`) is forbidden** — signatures
made with historic algorithms have *permanently failed* evaluation. **RFC 8463** adds
`ed25519-sha256` keys — but real-world verifier support is roughly half the market (Gmail returns
`dkim=neutral`, Microsoft 365 errors, Yahoo permfails on Ed25519), so Ed25519 is only safe
**dual-signed alongside RSA**. **RFC 8601** defines the `Authentication-Results` result values
(`pass`/`fail`/`policy`/`neutral`/`temperror`/`permerror`) our message-layer checks will parse.
M3AAWG guidance recommends **rotating keys at least every six months** (publish the new key ≥48 h
before signing with it; keep the old record 7–30 days after cutover, then revoke with an empty
`p=`).

**Why receivers care.** A verified DKIM signature is a stable, forward-surviving identity a receiver
can attach reputation to. It is one of the two authentication results DMARC aligns against
(`d=` domain vs the `From:` domain). Gmail, Yahoo, and Outlook all weight a valid DKIM signature
positively and treat its absence — for a bulk sender — as a reason to filter or reject.

**Common real-world failure modes.** The single most common failure is not a syntactically broken
record but a **selector that was never published** (the ESP was set up to sign, but the operator
never added the TXT/CNAME record at the registrar), so every message carries a `DKIM-Signature` that
fails to verify (`permerror "no key for signature"`) — worse than not signing at all. Close behind:
**legacy 1024-bit keys** never upgraded (history says this matters: in 2012 mathematician Zachary
Harris factored Google's then-512-bit DKIM key for ~$75 of cloud time and spoofed mail between the
founders — US-CERT VU#268267; Google moved to 2048-bit within days), a **`t=y` test flag left in
production** (receivers must treat mail as unsigned), an **empty `p=`** left behind after a key was
revoked, **CNAME delegations to an ESP whose target has since been removed** (dangling delegation →
NXDOMAIN → no key, and a subdomain-takeover risk), and **ESPs signing with their own default
domain** (`d=sendgrid.net`-style) — `dkim=pass` that contributes nothing to DMARC alignment.

**Newer attack surface this spec tracks.** The 2024 Zone.eu research showed the **`l=` body-length
tag** lets attackers append rendered content to signed mail while DKIM, DMARC — and even the BIMI
logo — keep passing; flag `l=` on sight (message layer, future). **DKIM replay** (re-broadcasting
one legitimately signed message at scale; the April 2025 `d=google.com` phishing wave is the
canonical case) is mitigated sender-side by short `x=` expirations and **oversigning** (listing
`From`/`To`/`Subject`/`Date` twice in `h=` so they cannot be added post-signing) — both future
message-layer sub-checks.

**How a failure degrades inbox placement.** No/invalid DKIM removes the forward-surviving half of
DMARC alignment; if SPF also fails to align (e.g. after a forward), the message fails DMARC entirely
and is quarantined/rejected under an enforcing policy. Even under `p=none`, receivers use DKIM
pass/fail as a reputation input, so unsigned or unverifiable mail lands in spam more often, and a
weak (1024-bit) or SHA-1 key is a negative trust signal in its own right.

## 2. Tests we perform

### 2.1 The four testing layers (methodology map)

Every way DKIM can be tested falls into one of four layers. This spec ships layer 1 + 2 now; layers
3 + 4 are future rounds that reuse the same `Checker`/`Finding` contract:

| Layer | What it answers | Instruments | Round |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1. DNS layer** | Is the published key correct — present, parseable, strong, sane flags, resolvable delegation? | `node:dns/promises`; `dig`/`doggo`/`kdig` as debug bench; `openssl` for key decode | **first round** |
| **2. Discovery layer** | Which selectors exist for this domain at all? | Configured selectors + MX-guided common-selector wordlist probing (`dnsx` for bulk) | **first round** |
| **3. Message layer** | Does a real signed message verify — `bh=`/`b=` pass, canonicalization sound, `h=` coverage/oversigning, no `l=`, sane `x=`, `d=` aligned with `From:`? | `swaks` probe send + **mailauth** (npm) verification; reflectors (`check-auth@verifier.port25.com`, dkimvalidator.com); Gmail "Show original" | future |
| **4. Observational layer** | What do real receivers report — per-source/per-selector DKIM pass rates, unsigned streams, pass-but-unaligned ESPs, replay anomalies? | DMARC `rua` aggregate reports via **parsedmarc** ([../emails.mdx](../emails.mdx)); `Authentication-Results` (RFC 8601) parsing | future |

### 2.2 Sub-checks

Every sub-check runs **per selector** unless noted as domain-scoped. Selectors come from
`CheckContext.dkimSelectors` (the per-domain selector list); when that list is empty,
`dkim.selector_discovery` probes the common-selector wordlist to find any that exist.

| Sub-check (`checkId`) | What it verifies | How measured | Typical critical / warning finding | Concrete remediation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `dkim.present` | For each configured selector, a TXT (or CNAME→TXT) record exists at `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>`. | `resolveTxt('<selector>._domainkey.<domain>')`; on NODATA try `resolveCname` then resolve the target. | **critical:** no record for a selector known to sign mail (`permerror "no key for signature"` at every receiver). | Publish the public key your ESP/MTA generated as a TXT record at `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` (or the CNAME your ESP specifies). |
| `dkim.parses` | The record is a valid key: optional `v=DKIM1`, a `k=` type, and a **non-empty `p=`** whose base64 decodes to a well-formed public key (DER SPKI for RSA; raw 32 bytes for Ed25519). | Parse tag=value pairs; base64-decode `p=`; `crypto.createPublicKey` on the SPKI. | **critical:** record does not parse / bad base64 (pasted PEM armor, smart quotes, injected line breaks are the classic causes). | Republish the exact key exported from the signing system — single logical string, no `-----BEGIN…` armor, no quotes-in-quotes, no newlines. |
| `dkim.revoked` | `p=` is not empty. An empty `p=` is a **valid revocation** (RFC 6376 §3.6.1) — fatal only if the selector still signs mail. | Detect empty `p=` during parse. | **critical:** empty `p=` on a configured (expected-to-sign) selector; **info:** empty `p=` on a discovered selector (proper decommissioning). | Point the signer at the new selector/key, or repopulate `p=` if the revocation was accidental. |
| `dkim.keylength` | RSA modulus is **≥ 1024 bits (required)** and **≥ 2048 bits (recommended)** per RFC 8301; Ed25519 is fixed-strength and passes. | Decode `p=`, read the RSA modulus bit length (or detect `k=ed25519`). | **critical:** < 1024-bit RSA (factorable — the 2012 Google incident); **warning:** exactly 1024-bit RSA (upgrade advised). | Generate a **2048-bit** RSA key, publish it on a new selector, re-sign, then retire the old selector. (Avoid 4096 — it breaks the 255-byte TXT chunking at some DNS hosts.) |
| `dkim.testflag` | The record is **not** in test mode. `t=y` tells verifiers to treat mail from the domain **as unsigned**. | Read the `t=` tag; look for the `y` flag. | **warning:** `t=y` present on a production selector. | Remove the `y` flag from the `t=` tag (or drop `t=` entirely) once testing is complete. `t=s` (strict subdomain flag) is separate — surface as `info` since it forbids subdomain `i=` identities. |
| `dkim.algorithm` | The key type / advertised hash is modern: `k=rsa` or `k=ed25519`; **no SHA-1**. The `h=` tag (acceptable hash algorithms), if present, must not restrict to `sha1`. | Read `k=` and the optional `h=` tag. | **critical:** `h=sha1` only (SHA-1 forbidden, RFC 8301 — permanent failure at modern verifiers); **warning:** unknown `k=` type. | Reissue signing with `rsa-sha256` (remove any `h=sha1`); optionally add an `ed25519-sha256` selector alongside RSA. |
| `dkim.ed25519_only` | The domain is not signing **only** with Ed25519. Gmail returns `neutral`, Microsoft 365 errors, and Yahoo permfails on Ed25519 — an Ed25519-only domain is effectively unsigned at the big three. | Domain-scoped: after all selectors resolve, check whether ≥1 healthy **RSA** key exists whenever any `k=ed25519` key exists. | **warning:** every working selector is `k=ed25519` (no RSA fallback). | **Dual-sign**: keep an RSA-2048 selector as primary and Ed25519 as secondary — verifiers accept a message if *any* signature validates. |
| `dkim.flags` | Tag sanity for the remaining RFC 6376 tags: `s=` (service type: `*` or `email`), `t=` flags (`y` test, `s` no-subdomain), deprecated `g=` (granularity) not overly restrictive, `n=` notes ignored, `h=` a valid hash list. | Tokenize all tags; validate values against the RFC 6376 grammar. | **warning:** `s=` excludes `email`; `g=` set to a value that won't match the signing identity; unknown/malformed tag. | Set `s=email` (or `s=*`); remove a restrictive/deprecated `g=`; fix or drop any malformed tag so verifiers accept the key. |
| `dkim.single_record` | Exactly **one** TXT record answers at the selector name. RFC 6376 §3.6.2.2: multiple TXT RRs at one selector → "the results are undefined" (and CNAME+TXT coexisting is illegal). | Count the TXT answers (after filtering to DKIM-looking strings, count all). | **warning:** ≥2 TXT records at one selector name (verifier behavior undefined — some pick the wrong one). | Delete the extra TXT record(s) so exactly one remains at the selector name. |
| `dkim.multi` | The domain has **≥ 1 working selector** and, ideally, a **second selector staged** so keys can rotate without an outage (the Microsoft 365 `selector1`/`selector2` pattern). | Count selectors that pass `present`+`parses`+`keylength`. | **warning:** exactly one working selector (no rotation headroom); **critical:** zero working selectors. | Publish a second selector now (even pre-activation) so the next rotation is a cutover, not an outage. |
| `dkim.rotation` | Key age vs the **6-month M3AAWG cadence**, when the selector's first-seen date is known (tracked in the store per `(selector, key_sha256)`). | Compare `first_seen_at` to `now`; config window default 180 days. | **warning:** key older than the rotation window; **info:** approaching it. | Rotate: publish a fresh key on a new selector ≥48 h ahead, switch signing, keep the old record 7–30 days, then revoke (empty `p=`). |
| `dkim.selector_discovery` | When no selectors are configured, probe the **common-selector wordlist** (§4, MX-guided) to find published keys the operator forgot to register. | Bounded-concurrency TXT/CNAME lookups over the wordlist. | **info:** discovered selector(s) not in config; **warning:** a discovered selector that is broken (weak/revoked). | Add discovered selectors to the domain's monitored selector list so they are audited every run; fix any that are broken. |
| `dkim.cname_delegation` | Selectors published as **CNAME to an ESP** (e.g. `s1._domainkey.<domain>` → `s1.domainkey.uXXXX.wlYYY.sendgrid.net`) resolve through to a valid key at the target. | `resolveCname` the selector name; then `resolveTxt` the CNAME target and parse it. | **critical:** CNAME target NXDOMAIN / no key (dangling delegation — ESP removed; also a takeover risk); **warning:** CNAME chain > 1 hop or target key weak. | Re-point the CNAME to the exact target your ESP lists (copy it from the ESP dashboard); remove the CNAME if that ESP is decommissioned. |
| `dkim.duplicate_key` | The **same public key (`p=`)** is not reused across multiple domains or multiple selectors within a domain (shared private key = shared blast radius + shared reputation). | Hash the decoded public key; compare across this run and the latest audit of every other monitored domain. | **warning:** identical `p=` seen on another domain/selector. | Generate a **unique key per domain** (and per rotation generation); never copy one private key across brands/domains. |
| `dkim.record_size` | The TXT record stays within DNS limits: each character-string ≤ 255 bytes and the total answer avoids UDP truncation (RSA-2048 must be split into multiple 255-byte strings; 4096-bit keys frequently break resolvers without EDNS0/TCP fallback). | Inspect the TXT chunk lengths returned by `resolveTxt`. | **warning:** a single string > 255 bytes (some providers reject), or an oversize record risking truncation → intermittent `temperror`. | Split the `p=` value into correctly-quoted ≤255-byte strings (most DNS UIs do this automatically); prefer 2048-bit RSA or add Ed25519 for a far shorter record. |
| `dkim.underscore_label` | The queried name is exactly `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` (the fixed `_domainkey` label present, selector not doubled or misspelled), and a wildcard TXT is not shadowing every selector. | String-validate the constructed query name; probe a random nonexistent selector — if it "resolves", a wildcard is answering. | **warning:** a wildcard `*.<domain>` TXT makes every selector "resolve" with non-DKIM data (false positives for us *and* `permerror` junk for receivers). | Ensure the key sits under the literal `_domainkey` label; remove or scope any wildcard TXT that shadows `_domainkey`. |
| `dkim.alignment` *(future — message/rua layer)* | The signing `d=` domain **aligns** with the visible `From:` domain for DMARC (relaxed = org-domain match). `dkim=pass header.d=sendgrid.net` contributes nothing to your DMARC. | Parse `d=` from probe-message signatures / `rua` `auth_results`. | **warning:** mail signs with an ESP default domain — pass-but-unaligned. | Complete the ESP's custom-domain authentication (SendGrid Domain Authentication, SES Easy DKIM, Mailgun sending domain) so `d=` becomes your domain. |
| `dkim.signature_verify` *(future — message layer)* | A **real sent message actually verifies**: `bh=` and `b=` check out; canonicalization (`c=relaxed/relaxed` preferred) survives transit; `h=` covers `From`/`To`/`Subject`/`Date`. | `swaks` probe send through the sender's MTA → verify with mailauth. | **critical:** signed message fails verification despite a healthy key (body-hash mismatch from footers/gateways; header rewrite under `c=simple`). | Sign as the *last* content-changing step; use relaxed/relaxed; stop modifying signed headers post-signing. |
| `dkim.length_tag` *(future — message layer)* | No **`l=` body-length tag** in observed signatures (2024 Zone.eu exploit: append rendered content while DKIM/DMARC/BIMI keep passing). | Parse `l=` from observed `DKIM-Signature` headers. | **critical:** any `l=` present. | Remove `l=` from the signer config — always sign the whole body. |
| `dkim.replay_hardening` *(future — message layer)* | Replay-mitigation hygiene: `x=` expiration present with a sane window (x − t between ~3 h and ~30 d; x > t required), and `From`/`To`/`Subject`/`Date` **oversigned** (listed twice in `h=`). | Parse `t=`/`x=`/`h=` from observed signatures. | **info/warning:** no `x=`, or no oversigning; **warning:** `x= ≤ t=` (invalid) or `t=` in the future (clock skew — NTP-sync the signer). | Add a short `x=` (re-sign deferred queue retries) and oversign the display-critical headers. |

## 3. Testing toolbox — Brew / npm / pipx tools

Verified against `formulae.brew.sh` and the npm/PyPI registries (2026-07): brew ships **doggo
1.2.0**, **dnsx 1.2.3**, **knot 3.5.5** (kdig), **checkdmarc 5.17.3**, **parsedmarc 10.2.0**,
**mailpit 1.30.3**, **swaks 20240103.0**, **dnsviz 0.11.1** (plus bind, ldns, q, massdns, msmtp,
openssl@3); npm ships **mailauth 4.13.3** (library + CLI); PyPI ships **dkimpy 1.1.8**
(`dkimverify`/`dkimsign`/`dknewkey`). First-round detection is pure `node:dns/promises`
(no binaries required); these tools are the **debug bench** we shell out to for deeper diagnosis,
cross-checking our own checker, and the future message layer. **Every tool invocation a run makes
is captured verbatim into `dkim.tool_runs[]`** in the run's YAML file (see *How a test run
executes the tools* below, and §5). Install the bench with:

```bash
brew install bind doggo q dnsx ldns knot dnsviz checkdmarc parsedmarc swaks msmtp mailpit openssl@3
npm install mailauth        # library + CLI, same TS/Node stack as the app
pipx install dkimpy         # dkimverify/dkimsign/dknewkey — minimal scriptable verifier
```

> **Key gap to know:** homebrew-core has **no DKIM-specific formula** — `opendkim`
> (`opendkim-testkey`), `rspamd` (`rspamadm dkim_keygen`), and `spamassassin` are all absent from
> brew (stale web guides notwithstanding). The message-layer verifier of choice is **mailauth**
> (npm) — it authenticates a raw `.eml` exactly like a receiving MTA (SPF, DKIM, DMARC, ARC, BIMI,
> alignment) and doubles as a backend **library** on our own Node stack.

### How a test run executes the tools

The **decision engine** stays `node:dns/promises` (deterministic, no binaries required). On top of
it, every run shells out to the bench tools below for **evidence-grade captures and cross-checks**,
and records **one entry per invocation** in `dkim.tool_runs[]` inside the run's YAML file
(`~/.email_delivery_hero/runs/<domain>/<timestamp>.yaml` — [../storage.mdx](../storage.mdx), §5):

```yaml
tool_runs:
  - tool: doggo                    # binary name
    command: "doggo s1._domainkey.example.com TXT --json --timeout 5s"  # exact argv string
    started_at: 2026-07-01T16:14:03Z
    duration_ms: 142
    exit_code: 0
    output_format: json            # json | text
    parsed: {…}                    # the captured/parsed stdout (shape per tool, below)
    error: null                    # null, or the failure string (timeout, ENOENT, bad JSON…)
```

Rules that hold for **every** invocation: `command` is the **exact argv string with every input
argument inlined** (selector, domain, resolver, file path) so a user can paste it into a terminal
and reproduce the capture; stdout is captured in full, parsed per the tool's format, and stored
under `parsed`; stderr goes into `error` on non-zero exit; each process gets a **hard kill
timeout** (per-tool below) beyond any tool-level timeout flag; a **missing binary** records the
entry with `exit_code: null` and `error: "ENOENT — not installed (brew install <formula>)"` and the
checker degrades to its `node:dns/promises` result for that step (the UI uses the error to offer
the install command). Tool runs never change a sub-test verdict on their own — they are evidence;
when a cross-check **disagrees** with the in-process result, the disagreement itself is surfaced
(propagation / resolver-dependent visibility warning).

Per tool, in execution order within a run:

| # | Tool | Exact command line (inputs inlined) | Output format | Timeout | Parsed how → feeds |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **doggo** — TXT fetch, once per selector | `doggo <selector>._domainkey.<domain> TXT --json --timeout 5s` | `json` | 5 s flag, 8 s kill | `JSON.parse(stdout)`; answers' `content` strings (joined 255-byte chunks) and TTLs → `selectors[].raw_record`, `txt_record_count`, TTL shown as evidence in `dkim.present.<selector>` |
| 2 | **doggo** — CNAME fallback, only when #1 returns no TXT | `doggo <selector>._domainkey.<domain> CNAME --json --timeout 5s` | `json` | 5 s flag, 8 s kill | first CNAME answer → `selectors[].resolved_via: cname`, `cname_target`; then #1 re-runs against the target (`dkim.cname_delegation`) |
| 3 | **kdig** — public-resolver cross-check, once per selector | `kdig @8.8.8.8 +json +timeout=5 +retry=1 TXT <selector>._domainkey.<domain>` | `json` (RFC 8427) | 5 s flag, 8 s kill | `answerRRs` compared with #1/`node:dns`; mismatch → propagation note on the `dkim.present.<selector>` evidence (never a fabricated critical) |
| 4 | **openssl** — key decode, once per parseable `p=` | `openssl pkey -pubin -inform DER -text -noout` (the base64-decoded `p=` bytes are piped on **stdin**; `parsed.stdin_source` records `"p= of <selector> (<n> bytes)"`) | `text` | 3 s kill | regex the first line — `RSA Public-Key: \((\d+) bit\)` or `ED25519 Public-Key` — cross-checks `key_type`/`key_bits` (primary decode is Node `crypto.createPublicKey`); non-zero exit → evidence on `dkim.parses.<selector>` |
| 5 | **doggo** — wildcard probe, once per domain | `doggo zz-<random8>._domainkey.<domain> TXT --json --timeout 5s` | `json` | 5 s flag, 8 s kill | any answer → `wildcard_shadow: true` (`dkim.underscore_label`), discovery results suppressed |
| 6 | **dnsx** — discovery sweep, only when no selectors configured | `dnsx -txt -cname -resp -json -silent -retry 1 -t 6` (candidate FQDNs `<sel>._domainkey.<domain>`, one per line, piped on **stdin**; `parsed.stdin_source` records `"wordlist (<n> names, MX-guided first)"`) | `json` (NDJSON) | 30 s kill (whole sweep) | `JSON.parse` per line; hits → `selectors[]` rows with `source: discovered` (`dkim.selector_discovery`) |

Future rounds append to the same array with the same shape: the message layer records
`npx mailauth report /path/to/probe.eml` (JSON report → `dkim.signature_verify`, `dkim.alignment`,
`l=`/`x=`/`h=` audits) and the `swaks` probe send; the observational layer records
`parsedmarc /path/to/report.xml.gz` invocations ([../emails.mdx](../emails.mdx)). `checkdmarc`
(no DKIM-selector probing) and the remaining §3 tools are **bench-only**: they appear in
drill-down "diagnose it yourself" blocks but are never invoked by a run, so they never appear in
`tool_runs[]`.

## 4. How it is measured

**Query construction.** For each selector `s` and domain `d`, the checker builds the FQDN
`` `${s}._domainkey.${d}` `` and calls `resolveTxt(name)` from `node:dns/promises`. `resolveTxt`
returns `string[][]` (an array of records, each an array of character-strings); the checker joins
the inner array with `''` (no separator — DKIM splits `p=` across 255-byte strings) before parsing.

**CNAME resolution (`dkim.cname_delegation`).** If `resolveTxt` throws `ENODATA`/`ENOTFOUND`, the
checker calls `resolveCname(name)`. If a CNAME target exists, it records `resolved_via = 'cname'`,
then `resolveTxt` the target and parses the key found there. This is the normal shape for SendGrid,
Mailchimp, Amazon SES, Postmark, Microsoft 365, etc. A CNAME with no resolvable target is a
**dangling delegation** → `critical`.

**Parsing.** The record is split on `;` into `tag=value` pairs (whitespace-trimmed, case-insensitive
tag names). Relevant tags: `v` (must be `DKIM1` if present), `k` (`rsa`|`ed25519`, default `rsa`),
`p` (base64 public key; **empty = revoked**), `t` (flags: `y`,`s`), `s` (service type), `h`
(acceptable hash algorithms), `g` (deprecated granularity), `n` (human note). The `p=` value is
base64-decoded; for RSA it is DER-parsed as a SubjectPublicKeyInfo and the **modulus bit length** is
read (Node `crypto.createPublicKey` on the decoded DER); for `k=ed25519` the key is a fixed
32-byte raw public key (always "strong" — but see `dkim.ed25519_only`).

**Selector discovery (`dkim.selector_discovery`).** Runs **only** when the domain has no configured
selectors. Two passes:

1. **MX-guided:** `resolveMx(domain)` first; the MX host names the provider, and the provider names
   the likely selectors — probe those first:

   | MX contains | Provider | Probe first |
   | --- | --- | --- |
   | `google.com` / `googlemail` | Google Workspace | `google` |
   | `protection.outlook.com` | Microsoft 365 | `selector1`, `selector2` |
   | `zoho` | Zoho Mail | `zoho`, `zmail` |
   | `mimecast` / `pphosted` / `barracuda` | gateway in front | fall through to the wordlist |

2. **Wordlist:** parallel TXT/CNAME lookups (bounded concurrency ~6, short timeout) over the curated
   common-selector list — generic first, then ESP conventions:
   `default`, `dkim`, `dkim1`, `dkim2`, `mail`, `email`, `smtp`, `selector`, `selector1`,
   `selector2`, `key1`, `key2`, `k1`, `k2`, `k3` (Mailchimp), `s1`, `s2` (SendGrid; legacy
   `smtpapi`), `s1024`, `mx`, `google` (Google Workspace), `mandrill`, `mte1`, `mte2` (Mailchimp
   Transactional), `mailjet`, `pm` (Postmark — real selectors are date-based `YYYYMMDDpm`), `hs1`,
   `hs2` (HubSpot), `km1`, `km2` (Klaviyo), `brevo1`, `brevo2` (Brevo/Sendinblue), `zendesk1`,
   `zendesk2`, `ctct1`, `ctct2` (Constant Contact), `zoho`, `zmail`, `protonmail`, `protonmail2`,
   `protonmail3`, `sig1` (Apple iCloud custom domains), `sfdc` (Salesforce convention),
   `everlytickey1`, `everlytickey2`, `pdk1` (Mailgun), `mailo`.

   Hits are `info` findings recommended for adding to config. It is deliberately a curated list (not
   a brute force) to stay polite to resolvers — Amazon SES's three random-token CNAME selectors and
   custom selectors escape any wordlist **by design**; the durable sources for those are pasted
   message headers and `rua` reports (layers 3–4). A selector *name* proves nothing about the
   provider — anyone can name a selector `google`.

**Duplicate-key detection (`dkim.duplicate_key`).** The checker computes `sha256(decoded p=)` and
compares it against the same hash from **other selectors in this run** and from the **most recent
audit of every other monitored domain** (read from the store). A collision across domains is a
shared private key → `warning`.

**Severity mapping.**

| Condition | Severity |
| --- | --- |
| No record for a configured signing selector; unparseable record; empty `p=` (revoked) on a configured selector; RSA < 1024-bit; SHA-1-only; dangling CNAME target; zero working selectors | **critical** |
| 1024-bit RSA; `t=y` in production; only one working selector; multiple TXT records at a selector; Ed25519-only; key past rotation window; duplicate key across domains; oversize record; wildcard shadowing; `s=` excludes email | **warning** |
| Discovered-but-unconfigured selector; approaching rotation window; `t=s` strict flag; empty `p=` on a merely-discovered selector; `n=` notes | **info** |
| Selector present, parses, ≥2048-bit RSA (or Ed25519 alongside RSA), no test flag, one TXT record, sane tags | **ok** |

**Edge cases.** (a) An empty `p=` is a **valid revocation**, not a parse error — `critical` only for
a configured selector (see `dkim.revoked`). (b) `resolveTxt` returning multiple TXT records at one
selector name → `dkim.single_record` warning; evaluate the first DKIM-parseable one. (c) A
**wildcard TXT** (`*.<domain>`) can make every probed selector "resolve" with junk —
`dkim.underscore_label` probes a random nonexistent selector to detect this and suppresses
discovery false-positives. (d) **Transient DNS failures** (`ESERVFAIL`/timeouts) must be surfaced
as `warning "could not determine"`, **never** as a `critical` "missing" — a resolver hiccup must not
fabricate a problem (important for the scheduler's regression diff). (e) Trailing-dot and case
normalization on the domain before building the name. (f) CNAME chains longer than one hop resolve
fine but are noted (fragility).

**Caching / rate limits.** Pure-DNS sub-checks are cheap; results honor the record TTL implicitly
via the OS resolver. Selector discovery is the only fan-out — cap concurrency (~6), short per-lookup
timeout, cache negative results within the run. No third-party rate limits until the future message
layer (reflectors and seed mailboxes have their own etiquette).

## 6. User interface — the summary surface and the DKIM category run page

Two surfaces, following the app-wide pattern in [../ui.mdx](../ui.mdx): a **summary cell** on the
Dashboard tables, and the **"view one category run" page** — a full page showing the DKIM sub-test

`/domains/:domainId`). The DKIM category run page is

### 6.1 The summary surface (Dashboard cells)

DKIM is one of the six locked test columns on **both** Dashboard tables
([../dashboard.mdx](../dashboard.mdx)). The DKIM cell is the **worst severity** among all `dkim.*`
sub-tests of the run the row represents — the domain's **newest** run on the Domain-health table,
**that** run on the Runs table — and shows the single most decision-relevant metric for this
technology: **working selectors and key strength**.

- **Color** — dark green (all selectors healthy, ≥2048-bit), amber (worst = warning, e.g. a
  1024-bit key or single selector), dark red (any critical — missing/revoked/SHA-1/dangling CNAME),
  light gray (never run). `info`-only findings (e.g. a discovered selector) keep the cell green.
- **Label** — the one-line summary: `2 selectors · 2048-bit` / `1 of 2 selectors failing` /
  `selector "s1" missing` / `No selectors` / `—`.
- **Hover** — tooltip with the top open problem's title.
- **Cell click (the one exception to whole-row navigation)** — clicking the **DKIM cell itself**
  goes *inside the DKIM test* of the run the row represents:
  - **Domain-health table** — opens the domain's **newest run's** DKIM page
    (`/domains/:domainId/dkim`, the newest-run alias).
  - **Runs table** — opens **that run's** DKIM page (`/domains/:domainId/runs/:runId/dkim`).
  - Clicking **anywhere else in a row** still opens the **whole run report**
    (`/domains/:domainId/runs/:runId`; Domain-health rows open the newest via
    `/domains/:domainId`), per [../dashboard.mdx](../dashboard.mdx) §6.

answer, top to bottom: *Was my mail signed with
healthy keys at this run? What exactly did we observe (the DNS names, the raw records, the key

8. **Message-layer panel (future)** — once the probe path lands: last probe verdict per receiver
   (verified ✓/✗, canonicalization, alignment, `l=`/`x=`/oversigning audit), and per-selector pass
   rates from `rua` ingestion.

**Empty / never-run / loading states.** **Never-run** (the newest-run alias for a domain with no
runs) → gray header cell, no run timestamp, prev/next hidden, and a "No runs yet — run checks" CTA
that starts the first run. **Run not found** (a stale/deleted `runId` in the URL) → "This run no
longer exists" with a link to the domain's newest DKIM page. **Loading** → skeleton header +
skeleton selector cards + skeleton table rows (no layout shift). *No selectors + discovery found
nothing* is **not** empty — it renders
the `dkim.no_selectors` info plus the "likely unsigned" warning state (PS-12) with the selector
editor front and center.

Placement rules and rationale:

- **Highest: the verdict.** The header row carries identity + status **+ run identity**: the back
  link returns to **this run's** report, the run timestamp names exactly which run is on screen
  (same time as the YAML filename), and the **‹ prev / next ›** pair steps through the domain's run
  history without leaving the DKIM category (disabled ends, keyboard `←`/`→`). The **key-health
  hero** directly under it is the one line a non-expert needs — how many selectors work, how strong
  the weakest key is, and the single next move. One accent CTA maximum on the page (the hero's
  "next step"). The stale-run banner renders only when a newer run exists, so the newest-run alias
  never shows it.
- **Second band: selector cards, two-up.** DKIM is inherently **per selector**, so the evidence
  surface is a card per selector, not one blob: name + verdict badge top-left, resolution chain and
  raw record beneath (monospace, copy button), and the vitals line (key age, flags, record count)
  at the card foot. Discovered-but-unconfigured selectors render with a dashed border and an
  **"Add to monitored"** inline action. On narrow widths cards stack.
- **Main table: sub-test results.** A table, not cards — users scan many rows; sort order is fixed
  fail → warn → info → pass (never alphabetical, never by selector). Each row: status icon (✗ red /
  ⚠ amber / ✓ green / ⓘ slate), finding id in small caps (per-selector ids keep their `.selector`
  suffix so the row is self-locating), plain-language title, right-aligned chevron `[v]` accordion.
  Expanded row shows *observed* (exact DNS name + raw answer + decoded key facts, monospace),
  *expected*, *why it matters* (one sentence), and the **Fix** strip: the exact TXT/CNAME to publish
  with `[copy fix]`. Copy buttons always copy the publishable value, never prose.
- **Evidence accordion: tool runs.** Collapsed by default (experts only): one row per
  `dkim.tool_runs[]` entry — tool, exact `command` in monospace with a copy button (paste-and-
  reproduce is the point), duration, exit code — expanding to the captured `parsed` output.
  Amber rows for `error != null`, carrying the error string and the `brew install` hint.
- **Config band: the selectors editor.** Below the work items because configuration is a
  lower-frequency act than triage — but it must be on *this* page (DKIM is the only category with a
  per-domain input). Chips with an × per selector, an add field, and the discovery action with its
  import flow. Edits apply to future runs only; the run on screen is immutable history.
- **Lowest: problem-state cards** — education and deep dives; up to three per row, title + one-line
  hook + chevron to `/domains/:domainId/runs/:runId/dkim/:problemId`. They repeat information the
  table already flagged on purpose: the table is for operators, the cards for learners.
- **Type & color:** shadcn/ui primitives (`Card`, `Table`, `Accordion`, `Badge`), lucide icons
  (`KeyRound`, `ShieldCheck`, `ShieldAlert`, `Info`, `ChevronRight`, `Copy`, `Wrench`). Severity
  colors identical to the Dashboard cells. Monospace (`font-mono text-xs`) for every DNS name, raw
  record, CNAME target, and hash.
- **Drill-down pages** (`/domains/:domainId/runs/:runId/dkim/:problemId`; newest-run alias

  loses context — then: **Concept** (what this state means, 2–3 paragraphs), **Your data** (the §9

  further-reading footer. Back chevron returns to this run's DKIM page anchor.

## 8. Recommendations — how we tell the user to fix DKIM

Every failing test carries its concrete one-line remediation (§2). Above the per-test fixes, the
page computes one **primary recommendation** — the hero CTA — from this precedence ladder (first
match wins):

| Current state | Primary recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| No selectors configured, discovery found nothing (PS-12) | Your mail is likely unsigned. Enable DKIM at your provider (Google Admin → Authenticate email; M365 Defender → DKIM; your ESP's Domain Authentication) and add the selector here. |
| No selectors configured, discovery found some | Import the discovered selector(s) so every future run audits them. |
| Any configured selector missing / dangling CNAME (PS-01) | Publish the exact record: for TXT, the key from your provider dashboard; for CNAME, the exact target the ESP lists. This selector is failing **every** message it signs. |
| Record unparseable / revoked-but-signing (PS-02/PS-03) | Republish the key exactly as exported — no PEM armor, no line breaks; or point the signer at the live selector. |
| SHA-1 restriction (PS-06) | Remove `h=sha1` / re-sign with `rsa-sha256` — SHA-1 signatures permanently fail at modern receivers. |
| Weak key (PS-04) | Rotate to 2048-bit RSA on a **new** selector, switch signing, then retire the old one. |
| `t=y` in production (PS-05) | Remove the test flag — until then receivers treat your mail as unsigned. |
| Ed25519-only (PS-11) | Add an RSA-2048 selector and dual-sign — Gmail/Microsoft/Yahoo cannot verify Ed25519 alone. |
| Multiple TXT / wildcard / oversize (PS-08) | Clean the DNS: one record per selector, no wildcard shadowing `_domainkey`, ≤255-byte strings. |
| Only 1 working selector (PS-09) | Stage a second selector now so the next rotation is a cutover, not an outage. |
| Key older than the rotation window (PS-09) | Rotate per M3AAWG: new key ≥48 h ahead → switch → keep old 7–30 days → revoke (empty `p=`). |
| Duplicate key across domains (PS-10) | Generate a unique key pair per domain — one compromise (or one bad neighbor) must not spoof every brand. |
| All healthy (PS-00) | Keep it healthy: rotation reminder on cadence, and (future) enable the message-layer probe to catch body-hash/alignment breakage DNS can't see. |

Additional standing recommendations surfaced as `info` when applicable: prefer **CNAME delegation**
to ESPs (they rotate keys for you); prefer aligned DKIM on **every** stream before DMARC `p=reject`
(DKIM survives forwarding; SPF does not); never "fix" a body-hash problem with `l=`; put
`*._domainkey` **revocation wildcards** (empty `p=`) on parked/non-sending domains alongside their
`p=reject` DMARC.

## 9. Problem states

The health situations a domain can be in for DKIM. Each maps from finding ids (detection is
automatic — per-selector ids are matched on their `dkim.<subcheck>` prefix), and each has a
deep-dive below and a drill-down UI page (§7). PS-00 is the healthy goal state; the rest are
unhealthy in descending typical severity. PS-13…PS-16 are future (message/rua layers) and listed so
the catalog is stable when those rounds land.

| Id | Problem state | Detected by | Severity |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PS-00 | Signed and healthy | all tests pass; ≥1 (ideally 2) working ≥2048-bit selectors | ok |
| PS-01 | Selector not published (permerror "no key") | `dkim.present`, `dkim.cname_delegation` | critical |
| PS-02 | Record unparseable / bad base64 | `dkim.parses` | critical |
| PS-03 | Revoked key still configured (empty `p=`) | `dkim.revoked` | critical |
| PS-04 | Weak RSA key (<2048-bit) | `dkim.keylength` | critical (<1024) / warning (1024) |
| PS-05 | Test mode left on (`t=y`) | `dkim.testflag` | warning |
| PS-06 | SHA-1 restriction (`h=sha1`) | `dkim.algorithm` | critical |
| PS-07 | Dangling / fragile CNAME delegation | `dkim.cname_delegation` | critical |
| PS-08 | DNS pathologies: multi-TXT, oversize, wildcard shadowing | `dkim.single_record`, `dkim.record_size`, `dkim.underscore_label` | warning |
| PS-09 | No rotation headroom / stale key | `dkim.multi`, `dkim.rotation` | warning |
| PS-10 | Shared key across domains | `dkim.duplicate_key` | warning |
| PS-11 | Ed25519-only signing | `dkim.ed25519_only` | warning |
| PS-12 | No selectors known — mail likely unsigned | `dkim.no_selectors` + empty discovery | warning |
| PS-13 | Signs but doesn't align (`d=` is the ESP's domain) *(future)* | `dkim.alignment` | warning |
| PS-14 | Verification failing in transit (body-hash / canonicalization) *(future)* | `dkim.signature_verify` | critical |
| PS-15 | Replay exposure (no `x=`, no oversigning) *(future)* | `dkim.replay_hardening` | warning |
| PS-16 | `l=` body-length tag present *(future)* | `dkim.length_tag` | critical |

### PS-00 — Signed and healthy

The goal state: every configured selector resolves to one parseable record with a ≥2048-bit RSA key
(optionally an Ed25519 sibling), no test flag, a second selector staged, keys inside the rotation
window, no key shared with another domain.
- **Data fields:** `working_selectors ≥ 1`, every `selectors[]` row `present`+`parses`,
  `key_bits ≥ 2048` (or `key_type: ed25519` alongside RSA), `has_test_flag=false`,
  `txt_record_count=1`, `duplicate_keys=[]`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** nothing to fix — `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` for a sanity
  glance; pipe `p=` through openssl (§3) and confirm `RSA Public-Key: (2048 bit)`.
- **Tools:** dig; the openssl decode one-liner.
- **More health metrics:** the scheduled-run regression diff (a selector going missing, `key_bits`
  dropping, `t=y` reappearing, a CNAME going dangling, a new cross-domain key collision); key age
  vs the 180-day window; (future) per-selector pass rates from `rua`.
- **UI:** all-green selector cards; the drill-down page explains *keeping* it healthy — rotation is
  perpetual, and DNS health today says nothing about body-hash breakage (that's the message layer).

### PS-01 — Selector not published (permerror "no key")

- **Concept:** mail signed with this selector carries a `DKIM-Signature` every receiver fails —
  `permerror "no key for signature"` — which is *worse* than not signing. Classic after ESP
  onboarding where the DNS record was never added, a selector typo, or signing that was rotated to
  a selector whose record was deleted.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].present=false`, `query_name`, `resolved_via`, `cname_target`
  (when the failure is a dangling delegation), DNS rcode in the finding detail.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` (empty) → cross-check a public
  resolver `kdig @8.8.8.8 +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` → `dig +short CNAME
  <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` to see whether a delegation exists and where it dies.
- **Tools:** dig/kdig/doggo; the ESP dashboard (it displays the exact record it expects).
- **More health metrics:** regression diff — a selector that was `present` last run and is missing
  now is a **new problem** toast; (future) `rua` rows showing `permerror` per selector.
- **UI:** red selector card with the resolution chain and where it broke; fix strip carries the
  exact TXT or CNAME to publish; drill-down explains selectors and why an unpublished one fails
  everything.

### PS-02 — Record unparseable / bad base64

- **Concept:** a record exists but verifiers can't extract a key: PEM `-----BEGIN…` armor pasted
  into DNS, smart quotes, injected line breaks/whitespace mid-key, truncated paste, malformed tags.
  Receivers return `permerror` ("key syntax error" / "bad base64") — same net effect as no key.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].parses=false`, `raw_record` (read it character-by-character),
  `txt_record_count`, `oversize_chunk`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** fetch the raw record; `echo '<p-value>' | base64 -d | openssl pkey -pubin
  -inform DER -text -noout` — openssl's error names the corruption.
- **Tools:** dig + openssl@3; `dkimverify` (pipx dkimpy) against a saved message for a second
  opinion.
- **More health metrics:** regression diff on `raw_record` changes.
- **UI:** the card shows the raw record with the unparseable span highlighted; fix strip = republish
  instructions; drill-down walks the classic paste corruptions one by one.

### PS-03 — Revoked key still configured (empty `p=`)

- **Concept:** RFC 6376 §3.6.1 — an empty `p=` **means the key is revoked**. That's the correct
  final step of a rotation; it's a critical problem only when the signer still uses the selector:
  every message hard-fails. Detection severity therefore depends on `source`: configured selector →
  critical; merely discovered → info (proper decommissioning).
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].is_revoked=true`, `source`, `raw_record`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` → `v=DKIM1; p=` ; then confirm
  whether live mail still signs with `s=<sel>` (message headers, ESP config).
- **Tools:** dig; Gmail "Show original" on a fresh outbound message (read the `s=` actually used).
- **More health metrics:** none — either re-key or retire.
- **UI:** red "revoked" badge on the card; drill-down explains rotation end-state vs accidental
  revocation and shows both paths.

### PS-04 — Weak RSA key (512/768/1024-bit)

- **Concept:** sub-1024-bit keys are practically factorable — in 2012 Zachary Harris factored
  Google's 512-bit key for ~$75 and spoofed founder-to-founder mail (US-CERT VU#268267); several
  receivers now refuse <1024 outright. 1024-bit meets the RFC floor but is below the 2048-bit
  standard and a negative trust signal.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].key_bits`, `key_type`, `first_seen_at` (old + weak usually travel
  together).
- **Diagnose/debug:** the openssl decode one-liner (§3) — the first output line is the verdict.
- **Tools:** dig + openssl@3; the provider's key-length setting (Google Admin lets you pick 2048).
- **More health metrics:** fleet-wide: the duplicate-key table often shows one weak key cloned
  across domains — fix both at once.
- **UI:** amber (1024) or red (<1024) key badge on the card; fix strip = the §8 rotation recipe;
  drill-down tells the 2012 story and the exact new-selector rotation steps.

### PS-05 — Test mode left on (`t=y`)

- **Concept:** `t=y` tells verifiers the domain is *testing* — RFC 6376 requires them to treat mail
  as unsigned for policy purposes. The signature buys nothing: no DMARC credit, no reputation.
  Don't confuse with `t=s` (strict — forbids subdomain `i=` identities; info only).
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].has_test_flag`, `has_strict_flag`, `flags.t`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain> | grep -o 't=[^;]*'`.
- **Tools:** dig; the DNS console (the flag is one tag edit).
- **More health metrics:** regression diff — `has_test_flag` flipping back to true (a provider
  "reset" is the usual culprit) is flagged as a new problem.
- **UI:** amber flag chip on the card; fix strip carries the corrected record with `t=y` removed.

### PS-06 — SHA-1 restriction (`h=sha1`)

- **Concept:** RFC 8301 forbids SHA-1 in DKIM — signatures identified as rsa-sha1 have
  *permanently failed* evaluation at modern verifiers. A key record with `h=sha1` restricts the key
  to a dead algorithm.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].flags.h`, `key_type`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain> | grep -o 'h=[^;]*'`; message-side,
  check `a=` in a real `DKIM-Signature` header (`a=rsa-sha1` = same problem at the signer).
- **Tools:** dig; (future) mailauth reports the signature algorithm per message.
- **More health metrics:** none — remove and re-sign.
- **UI:** red badge; fix strip = record with `h=sha1` dropped and signer set to `rsa-sha256`.

### PS-07 — Dangling / fragile CNAME delegation

- **Concept:** delegated selectors (SendGrid/M365/Mailchimp/SES style) die when the ESP account is
  closed or migrated: the CNAME stays, the target vanishes → NXDOMAIN → `permerror` — and a stale
  vendor CNAME is a **subdomain-takeover** risk (someone re-registers the target and publishes
  *their* key under your name).
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].resolved_via=cname`, `cname_target`, `present=false`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short CNAME <sel>._domainkey.<domain>` → `dig +short TXT <target>` —
  find the hop that dies; compare the target against the ESP dashboard's current value.
- **Tools:** dig/doggo (follows chains); the ESP dashboard.
- **More health metrics:** regression diff on `cname_target` changes (an ESP-side migration shows up
  here before mail breaks).
- **UI:** the card renders the chain hop-by-hop with the dead hop struck through; fix strip = the
  exact current target to re-point at (or "remove — ESP decommissioned").

### PS-08 — DNS pathologies: multi-TXT, oversize, wildcard shadowing

- **Concept:** three record-shape faults that break verification sideways: (a) **multiple TXT
  records** at one selector — RFC 6376 §3.6.2.2 says results are *undefined*; (b) **oversize
  strings** (>255 bytes per character-string, or 4096-bit keys tripping UDP truncation) →
  intermittent `temperror`; (c) a **wildcard TXT** answering every `_domainkey` query with junk —
  receivers permerror, and naive discovery tools "find" selectors that don't exist.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].txt_record_count`, `oversize_chunk`, `wildcard_shadow` (domain
  level).
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain> +multiline` (count answers, see string
  splits); `dig +tcp` vs UDP to expose truncation; `dig +short TXT zz-random._domainkey.<domain>`
  (an answer = wildcard pollution).
- **Tools:** dig; dnsx for sweeping candidates.
- **More health metrics:** `temperror` clusters at one receiver (often Microsoft) in future `rua`
  data correlate with (b).
- **UI:** each fault renders as its own test row with the exact observed answers; drill-down
  explains all three shapes with their dig commands.

### PS-09 — No rotation headroom / stale key

- **Concept:** one selector and a years-old key means the next rotation is an outage and the
  private key has accumulated exposure (backups, tickets, ex-employees, breached ESPs). M3AAWG:
  rotate at least every 6 months; the dual-selector pattern (à la M365 `selector1`/`selector2`)
  makes rotation a cutover.
- **Data fields:** `working_selectors`, `selectors[].first_seen_at` (per `(selector, key_sha256)` —
  age survives across runs), date-embedded selector names as a freebie signal.
- **Diagnose/debug:** count working selectors on the page; check `first_seen_at` vs the 180-day
  window; CNAME-delegated selectors rotate at the ESP — confirm in their dashboard.
- **Tools:** the app's own run history is the age instrument; `dknewkey` (dkimpy) or the provider
  console to mint the next key.
- **More health metrics:** age trend per selector; the `info` "approaching window" finding fires
  before the warning.
- **UI:** the hero shows rotation headroom; the drill-down carries the M3AAWG rotation runbook
  (publish ≥48 h ahead → switch → 7–30 days grace → revoke).

### PS-10 — Shared key across domains

- **Concept:** the same public key on several domains means one private key signs them all — one
  compromise spoofs every brand, and reputation bleeds across tenants. Common when an operator
  copies a working key pair to a new domain, or an ESP issues one key for many customers.
- **Data fields:** `duplicate_keys[]` (`key_sha256` → list of `domain/selector` sightings),
  `selectors[].key_sha256`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** the app compares hashes across all monitored domains automatically; manually,
  decode `p=` from both records and diff.
- **Tools:** the duplicate-key table on the page; openssl for manual confirmation.
- **More health metrics:** new collisions flagged by the regression diff.
- **UI:** the card links the sibling domain(s); fix strip = mint a unique key per domain.

### PS-11 — Ed25519-only signing

- **Concept:** RFC 8463's Ed25519 is smaller and faster — but only about half of verifiers support
  it. Gmail returns `dkim=neutral` (treats the key as nonexistent), Microsoft 365 errors as
  malformed, Yahoo permfails. Ed25519-*only* signing is effectively unsigned at the big three.
- **Data fields:** `selectors[].key_type` per selector; the domain-level check (any healthy RSA?).
- **Diagnose/debug:** `dig +short TXT <sel>._domainkey.<domain> | grep -o 'k=[^;]*'` per selector;
  message-side, look for a second `DKIM-Signature` with `a=rsa-sha256`.
- **Tools:** dig; (future) mailauth shows both signatures' verdicts per message.
- **More health metrics:** receiver-support tracking is external — revisit annually.
- **UI:** slate `ed25519` badge, amber domain banner "no RSA fallback"; fix strip = add an RSA-2048
  selector and dual-sign (verifiers accept if *any* signature validates).

### PS-12 — No selectors known — mail likely unsigned

- **Concept:** we can't audit what we can't name, and if discovery finds nothing either, the domain
  is probably not signing at all — which since 2024 (Gmail/Yahoo) and May 2025 (Outlook) means
  filtered or rejected bulk mail. Alternatively the domain signs with an uncataloguable selector
  (SES random tokens, custom names) that headers or `rua` will reveal.
- **Data fields:** `selectors_configured=[]`, `discovery_ran=true`, zero discovered selectors,
  `wildcard_shadow=false` (a wildcard invalidates the "nothing found" conclusion).
- **Diagnose/debug:** send yourself a message and read `DKIM-Signature:` `s=`/`d=` in Gmail "Show
  original" — the definitive selector source; check the provider's admin console for DKIM status.
- **Tools:** Gmail "Show original"; the ESP dashboard; `dnsx` if you want to sweep a larger private
  wordlist.
- **More health metrics:** none until a selector is known.
- **UI:** the selectors editor is the hero on this page state; drill-down explains how to find the
  selector from headers step-by-step (screenshots), then enable DKIM per major provider.

### PS-13 — Signs but doesn't align *(future — message/rua layer)*

- **Concept:** the ESP signs with its own domain (`d=sendgrid.net`, `d=amazonses.com`,
  `d=mailchimpapp.net`) — `dkim=pass` at the receiver but **zero DMARC contribution**, because
  DMARC needs `d=` to align with the `From:` org-domain. The most common "everything passes but
  DMARC fails" confusion; invisible to pure-DNS auditing.
- **Data fields (future):** per-message `d=` vs `From:` org-domain; `rua` rows where raw
  `dkim=pass` but `policy_evaluated.dkim=fail`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** `Authentication-Results: dkim=pass header.d=sendgrid.net` while `From:` is
  your domain = this state; complete the ESP's custom-domain authentication.
- **Tools:** mailauth (`.dkim.results[].signingDomain`); parsedmarc; learndmarc.com.
- **UI:** message-layer panel row per stream: signer `d=`, aligned ✓/✗, and the ESP-specific fix
  link.

### PS-14 — Verification failing in transit *(future — message layer)*

- **Concept:** key healthy, DNS healthy — but real messages fail: **body-hash mismatch** (`bh=`;
  footers/disclaimers appended after signing, gateway URL-rewriting, MIME re-encoding, mailing
  lists) or **signature failure** (`b=`; signed headers rewritten — "[EXTERNAL]" subject taggers —
  or brittle `c=simple` canonicalization).
- **Diagnose/debug:** probe send → mailauth verdict; differential test direct vs through the
  suspect gateway; check `c=` (want `relaxed/relaxed`) and the `h=` list vs headers the path
  modifies.
- **Tools:** swaks + mailauth; reflectors; Gmail "Show original".
- **Fix:** sign as the last content-changing step; `relaxed/relaxed`; tag `[EXTERNAL]` on inbound
  only; never `l=`.

### PS-15 — Replay exposure *(future — message layer)*

- **Concept:** one legitimately signed message re-broadcast at scale keeps passing DKIM+DMARC (no
  envelope binding) and burns the signing domain's reputation — the April 2025 `d=google.com`
  OAuth-phishing wave is the canonical case. Sender-side hygiene: short `x=` (re-sign deferred
  retries) and oversigning `From`/`To`/`Subject`/`Date`.
- **Diagnose/debug:** parse `t=`/`x=`/`h=` from observed signatures; watch `rua` for
  volume spikes of passing mail from foreign sources.
- **Tools:** mailauth; parsedmarc.

### PS-16 — `l=` body-length tag present *(future — message layer)*

- **Concept:** `l=N` signs only the first N body bytes; the 2024 Zone.eu research showed attackers
  can append rendered content (even whole MIME parts) while DKIM, DMARC, and the BIMI logo keep
  passing. Never use it; several providers now treat `l=`-signed mail as unsigned.
- **Diagnose/debug:** grep observed `DKIM-Signature` headers for `l=`.
- **Fix:** remove `l=` from the signer; fix footer ordering properly (PS-14), never with `l=`.

## 10. Information schema (database schema)

DKIM reuses the **shared core tables** owned by engineering — do not redefine them. It **reads**
the per-domain selector list (which drives `CheckContext.dkimSelectors`) and writes `findings` like
every check. It adds **one structured result table**.

Shared core (reference only — defined in [Engineering](../engineering.mdx)):

```sql
-- domains(id, domain UNIQUE, scheduled, created_at, updated_at)
-- sending_ips(id, domain_id FK, ip inet, created_at)
-- dkim_selectors(id, domain_id FK, selector text, created_at)      -- the selectors this check probes
-- audit_runs(id, domain_id FK, started_at, finished_at, score, status, trigger)
-- findings(id, audit_run_id FK, domain_id FK, category, check_id, severity, title, detail, remediation, created_at)
```

Check-specific result table — **one row per selector per audit run**:

```sql
CREATE TABLE dkim_check_results (
    id              BIGSERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    audit_run_id    BIGINT NOT NULL REFERENCES audit_runs(id)   ON DELETE CASCADE,
    domain_id       BIGINT NOT NULL REFERENCES domains(id)      ON DELETE CASCADE,
    selector        TEXT   NOT NULL,                 -- e.g. 's1', 'google' (may be discovered)
    source          TEXT   NOT NULL DEFAULT 'configured', -- 'configured' | 'discovered'
    query_name      TEXT   NOT NULL,                 -- '<selector>._domainkey.<domain>'
    resolved_via    TEXT   NOT NULL DEFAULT 'txt',   -- 'txt' | 'cname' | 'none'
    cname_target    TEXT,                            -- populated when resolved_via = 'cname'
    raw_record      TEXT,                            -- full TXT string as fetched (NULL if absent)
    dkim_version    TEXT,                            -- v= tag ('DKIM1' or NULL/default)
    key_type        TEXT,                            -- k= tag: 'rsa' | 'ed25519'
    key_bits        INT,                             -- RSA modulus bits; NULL for ed25519/unparseable
    key_sha256      CHAR(64),                        -- sha256 of decoded public key (duplicate detect)
    has_test_flag   BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,  -- t=y present
    has_strict_flag BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,  -- t=s present
    is_revoked      BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,  -- p= empty
    txt_record_count INT   NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,       -- answers at the selector name (want exactly 1)
    oversize_chunk  BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,  -- any character-string > 255 bytes
    flags           JSONB  NOT NULL DEFAULT '{}',    -- {t:['y'], s:'email', h:['sha256'], g:'*', ...}
    first_seen_at   TIMESTAMPTZ,                     -- earliest audit that saw this key (rotation age)
    present         BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,
    parses          BOOLEAN NOT NULL DEFAULT FALSE,
    created_at      TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
    CONSTRAINT dkim_results_resolved_via_ck CHECK (resolved_via IN ('txt','cname','none')),
    CONSTRAINT dkim_results_source_ck       CHECK (source IN ('configured','discovered')),
    CONSTRAINT dkim_results_unique_sel UNIQUE (audit_run_id, selector)
);

CREATE INDEX dkim_results_domain_idx   ON dkim_check_results (domain_id, created_at DESC);
CREATE INDEX dkim_results_run_idx      ON dkim_check_results (audit_run_id);
CREATE INDEX dkim_results_keyhash_idx  ON dkim_check_results (key_sha256);  -- cross-domain dup lookup
```

Notes: `flags JSONB` holds the long tail of RFC 6376 tags without schema churn; `key_sha256` is the
join key for `dkim.duplicate_key` (indexed for the cross-domain compare); `first_seen_at` is carried
forward from the previous run's row for the same `(domain_id, selector, key_sha256)` so
`dkim.rotation` can compute age even though each run inserts a fresh row.

**Mapping onto today's file store.** There is **no database in the first round** (see
[Engineering §7](../engineering.mdx)). Today the structured observation is the `dkim` key of the
run's YAML file — `~/.email_delivery_hero/runs/<domain>/<YYYY_MM_DD_hh_mm{AM|PM}>.yaml`
([../storage.mdx](../storage.mdx)) — exactly the `selectors[]` +
`wildcard_shadow` + `duplicate_keys` + `tool_runs[]` + `tests[]` shape shown in §5 (same
snake_case keys in code, like
`results.dmarc`). The configured
selectors live on each domain record (`dkimSelectors: string[]` — the future `dkim_selectors`
table); `key_sha256` values for the duplicate-key compare are read across the latest stored audit of
each domain. Because everything is isolated behind the `store` module, the file→Postgres move is a
single-module change: `dkim_check_results` ← the `results.dkim.selectors[]` arrays,
`dkim_selectors` ← the domains' `dkimSelectors`.

## 11. When we run the check

**On-demand.** Runs as part of a full audit via `POST /api/domains/:id/audit` (per domain) or the
scan-all action. The DKIM checker executes in parallel with the other first-round checkers in the
runner; it is pure DNS so it adds only a few lookups per selector.

**Periodic.** The in-process scheduler re-runs the full engine per domain on the configured cadence
(default every 6h; see [Scheduled checks](../scheduled_checks.mdx)). DKIM state changes slowly, so a
6–24h cadence is ample; the value of frequent runs is catching an **operator error** (a selector
deleted, a key swapped to a weak one, a `t=y` reintroduced, an ESP silently rotating a CNAME
target) quickly.

**First-round vs future dependency.** Every §2 sub-check in layers 1–2 is first-round pure
`node:dns/promises` and ships now. Layer-3 (`dkim.signature_verify`, `dkim.length_tag`,
`dkim.replay_hardening`, `dkim.alignment` via probe) is gated behind the `swaks` + mailauth path;
layer-4 needs `rua` ingestion ([../emails.mdx](../emails.mdx)).

**Cost / rate-limit / back-off.** No third-party quotas for the DNS path. The only fan-out is
`dkim.selector_discovery`: bounded concurrency (~6), short timeout, curated wordlist (not brute
force), negative results cached within the run. Treat `ESERVFAIL`/timeout as
retry-once-then-`warning "could not determine"`, never as a fabricated "missing" `critical`.

**Regression detection.** The scheduler diffs each run against the previous (Engineering §8). DKIM
regressions to surface as **new problems**: a selector that was `present`+`ok` last run now
missing/revoked (green→red); `key_bits` dropping (2048→1024); `has_test_flag` flipping true; a CNAME
target changing or going dangling; `txt_record_count` rising above 1; a `key_sha256` newly colliding
with another domain.

## 13. Acceptance criteria

1. For each selector in `CheckContext.dkimSelectors`, the checker queries
   `<selector>._domainkey.<domain>` and emits `dkim.present.<selector>` (`critical` when absent for
   a configured signing selector).
2. A record whose `p=` is empty yields `dkim.revoked.<selector>` — `critical` for a configured
   selector, `info` for a merely-discovered one; a record whose `p=` does not base64-decode/DER-parse
   yields `dkim.parses.<selector>` `critical`.
3. RSA modulus is decoded and `dkim.keylength` is `critical` for < 1024-bit, `warning` for exactly
   1024-bit, `ok` for ≥ 2048-bit; `k=ed25519` passes `keylength` as `ok`.
4. A domain whose only working key(s) are Ed25519 yields `dkim.ed25519_only` `warning` with the
   dual-signing remediation.
5. `t=y` produces `dkim.testflag` `warning`; `t=s` produces an `info` note (not a warning).
6. `h=sha1`-only produces `dkim.algorithm` `critical` (SHA-1 forbidden per RFC 8301).
7. Two or more TXT records at one selector name produce `dkim.single_record` `warning` (RFC 6376
   §3.6.2.2 undefined behavior), and evaluation proceeds on the first parseable DKIM record.
8. A selector published as a CNAME resolves through to the target key; a dangling CNAME target
   yields `dkim.cname_delegation` `critical`; `resolved_via` is recorded as `cname` with
   `cname_target` set.
9. When `CheckContext.dkimSelectors` is empty, `dkim.selector_discovery` probes MX-guided +
   wordlist selectors with bounded concurrency and reports hits as `info`, offering to add them to
   config; a wildcard-TXT domain (random-selector probe answers) suppresses discovery results and
   yields `dkim.underscore_label` `warning`.
10. `dkim.duplicate_key` flags identical `sha256(p=)` across two monitored domains (or two selectors
    of one domain) as `warning`, naming the sibling.
11. A domain with exactly one working selector yields `dkim.multi` `warning`; zero working selectors
    = `critical`.
12. `dkim.rotation` computes key age from `first_seen_at` (carried across runs per
    `(selector, key_sha256)`) and warns past the configured window (default 180 days).
13. Every non-`ok` DKIM finding carries a **concrete** remediation string (an exact TXT value to
    publish, an exact `t=`/`k=`/`h=` edit, or the exact CNAME target to set) — never generic.
14. The checker returns a `CheckOutcome` whose `results` payload matches §5's `selectors[]` +
    `wildcard_shadow` + `duplicate_keys` shape, one entry per probed selector, and it is persisted
    as `results.dkim` on the audit run.
15. Transient DNS failures (`ESERVFAIL`/timeout) never produce a `critical` "missing" finding; they
    surface as a `warning "could not determine"` so the scheduler's regression diff is not poisoned.
16. The DKIM Dashboard cell shows the worst DKIM severity and a working-selectors/key-strength
    label, and exposes a **chevron** that navigates to `/domains/:id/dkim`; an `info`-only result
    does not turn the cell amber.
17. The full DKIM page renders (top to bottom) the key-health hero, per-selector cards with the raw
    record/resolution chain/key badges, the fail-first test-results table with observed evidence and
    copyable fixes, the selectors editor with discovery import, and matched problem-state cards
    linking to `/domains/:id/dkim/:problemId` drill-downs per §7.
18. Each §9 problem state PS-01…PS-12 is matched from finding-id prefixes and its drill-down page
    renders concept, live data fields, diagnose-it-yourself commands (§3 tools), and the numbered
    path forward.
